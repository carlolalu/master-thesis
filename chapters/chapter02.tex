The first part of this chapter will define a toy model of reality.
A key difference will be that the amoeba will release a single cAMP with no initial velocity for each spawning moment.
The second important difference is that the release of cAMP will be regulated by a Poisson process, thus ignoring the fact that in reality the amoebas should respond to each trace of cAMP with the release of a chemical cry of more chemotaxin.
The second part of the chapter is concerned with the definition of an alternative version of this first design, which can be handy for certain mathematical operations.
Recall that the space is throughout the whole master thesis $\R^d$ with $d\in\{2,3\}$, thus not the phase space.

\section{The first model}

In words we can describe this design in this way:
\begin{enumerate}
\item There is a fixed number $N$ of amoebas, each subjected to its own Brownian motion with constant diffusion coefficient $\sigma_\amba$
\item Each amoeba $\amba^p$ attracted to the cAMP molecules with a potential $\potential$ which has properties [[PROPERTIES MODELLED ON THE GAUSSIAN]]
\item Each amoeba $\amba^p$ spawns cAMP molecules $\camp^{p,g}$ (p=parent, g=generation) with a Poisson process $\hmactive^p$ of parameter $\lambda$ indipendent of $p$
\item Each cAMP molecule is born at the position of its parent and is subjected from that time on to its own Brownian motion with constant diffusion coefficient $\sigma_\camp$
\item All the Brownian motions for each particle are indipendent from each others
\end{enumerate}

Let us see all of this in equations. Let $N\in\N$ be the number of amoebas, and fix $N$ indipendent Poisson processes $(\hmactive^p)_{i=1}^N$ of parameter $\lambda$.
Define for each parent $p \in \{1,\ldots,N\}$ and each generation $g \in \N$, the stopping time

\begin{equation}
\btime^{p,g} := \inf \{ t : \hmactive^p_t \geq g \}
\end{equation}

\ie the $\Gma_{g, \lambda}$ distributed \emph{spawning} or \emph{birth time} of the $\camp^{p,g}$ cAMP molecule.

Consider a countable collection of indipendent Brownian motions 

\begin{equation}
\{B^{A,p}, B^{C,p,g}\}_{1\leq p\leq N,g\in\N} =:\{B^p, B^{p,g}\}_{p,g}
\end{equation}

\ie $N$ of them for the amoebas and a countable amount for the cAMP molecules.
Now we need an important passage to accurately define the Brownian movement of the cAMP particles.
In fact each of these molucules moves only after spawning, and thus such movement must be calibrated for starting after this moment, and not before.

\begin{definition}
Let $T$ be a stopping time and $B$ a Brownian motion.
Consider the process
\begin{equation}
B_t^{(T)} := B_{T+t} - B_T
\end{equation}
which is a \Bm (see for example \citep[Theorem 2.20]{le-gall}).
Then the \emph{Quasi Brownian Motion} $\quasibm_t^{B,T}$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\quasibm_t^{B,T} := "B_{t-T}^{(T)}\1_{t-T\geq 0}"
=
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
& 0 
& t<T
\\
& B_{t-T}^{(T)} = B_{t} - B_{T}
& t \geq T
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}\label{ch2:def:quasi-bm}

Then in our case, since $\forall p,g$ a unique \Bm and birht time is identified, we can write

\begin{equation}
\quasibm_t^{p,g} := \quasibm_t^{B^{p,g}, T^{p,g}}
= "B_{t-\btime^{p,g}}^{p,g,(\btime^{p,g})}\1_{t-\btime^{p,g}\geq 0}"
\end{equation}

which is lighter on notation.
With these indipendent processes we can now finally write down the model as a set of Poisson processes and stochastic differential equations:

\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
(\hmactive^p_t)_{p=1}^N 
&\text{ iid and } \sim \Pois_{\lambda t}
&
\\
\btime^{p,g} 
&:= \inf \{ t : \hmactive^p_t \geq g \}
&
\\
\amba_t^p 
&= \amba_0^p 
+ \int_0^t \frac{1}{N} \sum _{p = 1}^{N} \sum_{g = 1}^{\hmactive_s^p} \attraction(\amba_s^p - \camp_s^{p,g}) d s
+ 
\overbrace{\sigma^A B_t^p}^{\int_0^t \sigma_A d B_s^p}
&
\\
\camp_t^{p,g} 
&\qquad\text{does not exist} 
&\text{if} \quad t < \btime^{p,g}
\\
\camp_t^{p,g}
&= \amba_{\btime^{p,g}}^p + \underbrace{\sigma_C \quasibm_t^{p,g}}_{\int_0^t \sigma_C Q_s^{p,g}} 
&\text{if} \quad t \geq \btime^{p,g}
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}\label{ch2:model:1}

\paragraph{Observations} One can immediately notice that not even the dimension of the system \ref{ch2:model:1} is deterministic: the particles $\camp_t^{p,g}$ are spawning at the stochastic time $\btime^{p,g}$, increasing at each of these instants the number of particles involved (\ie the system is piecewise defined in time, according to stochastic times).
This, together with the fact that the Poisson processes $\hmactive^p$ cannot be represented with a SDE and that the $Q_t^{p,g}$ are not proper Brownian motions, makes this system formally not a system of SDEs, even though the equations involved are indeed differential and stochastic.

\subsection{Alternative formulation}

We can build a mathematically handier version of the previous model.
Since the Poisson processes $M^p$s determining the spawning times of particles are independent for each amoeba, we have that

\begin{equation}
\hmactive_t := \sum_{p=1}^N \hmactive_t^p
\end{equation}

is $\Pois(N t \lambda)$-distributed. Therefore the correspondent birth times

\begin{equation}
\btime_m := \inf \{ t : \hmactive_t \geq m\}
\end{equation}

are $\Gma(m, N\lambda)$-distributed.
Consider these as spawning times and re-indicise the cAMP particles consequently (\ie $\camp^m$ is the $m$-th spawned molecule, born at time $\btime_m$).
Then which amoeba $\amba^p$ is spawning $\camp^m$?

\begin{definition}
Define the random function $\Parentof : \N \longrightarrow \{1, \ldots, N\}$ as
\begin{equation}
\Parentof(m) := \{ p\; : \; A^p \text{ is the parent of } \camp^m \}
\end{equation}
This function is called \emph{parenting function}, and for each $m\in\N$ the random variable $\Parentof(m)$ is uniformly distirbuted on the amoebas $\{1, \ldots, N\}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proof}
\begin{align*}
\Prb (\Parentof(m) = p) 
&= \Prb (\exists g \in \N. \btime_m = \btime^{p,g} )
\\
&= \sum_{g=1}^{\infty} \Prb (\btime_m = \btime^{p,g})
\\
&= \sum_{g=1}^{\infty} \Prb (\hmactive_{\btime^{p,g}} = m)
\\
&= \sum_{g=1}^{\infty} \Prb (\hmactive_{\btime^{p,g}}^p = g, \sum_{k\neq p} \hmactive_{\btime^{p,g}}^k = m - g)
\\
&= \sum_{g=1}^{\infty} \Prb (g = g, \sum_{k\neq p} \hmactive_{\btime^{p,g}}^k = m - g)
\\
&= \sum_{g=1}^{\infty} \int \Prb( \sum_{k\neq p} \hmactive_{\btime^{p,g}}^k = m - g) 
	\big| \btime^{p,g} = s) d\Prb(\btime^{p,g} = s)
\\
&= \sum_{g=1}^{\infty} \int \Pois_{(N-1)s\lambda}(\{ m-g\})\1_{m-g \geq 0} 
	\underbrace{d\Prb(\btime^{p,g} = s)}_{:=\Zhi(p,g,s) ds}
\end{align*}

And since $\Zhi(p,g,s)ds=d\Gma_{g,\lambda} (s)$, it is independent from $p$, we have that $\Prb (\Parentof(m) = p) = const(m)$ and thus uniformly distirbuted on $\{1,\ldots,N\}$.
\end{proof}

Then we can reformulate the model as:

\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
(\hmactive^p_t)_{p=1}^N 
&\text{ iid and } \sim \Pois_{\lambda t}
,\: \hmactive_t := \sum_{p=1}^N \hmactive_t^p
&
\\
\btime_m
&:= \inf \{ t : \hmactive_t \geq m \}
&
\\
\amba_t^p 
&= \amba_0^p 
+ \int_0^t \frac{1}{N} \sum _{m = 1}^{\hmactive_s} \attraction(\amba_s^p - \camp_s^m) d s
+ 
\overbrace{\sigma^A B_t^p}^{\int_0^t \sigma_A d B_s^p}
&
\\
\camp_t^m 
&\qquad\text{does not exist} 
&if \quad t < \btime^m
\\
\camp_t^m
&= \amba_{\btime^m}^{\Parentof(m)} + \underbrace{\sigma_C \quasibm_t^m}_{\int_0^t \sigma_C Q_s^m}
&if \quad t \geq \btime^m
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}\label{ch2:model:1-alternative}

This makes the equations simpler and let us arrange the spawning time chronologically, a feature which is important to show that the system has a solution.
