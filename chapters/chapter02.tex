This chapter will define a toy model of reality and prove that it has a solution.
A key difference with the real situation will be that the amoeba will release a single cAMP with no initial velocity for each spawning moment.
The second important difference is that the release of cAMP will be regulated by a Poisson process, thus ignoring the fact that in reality the amoebas should respond to each trace of cAMP with the release of a chemical cry of more chemotaxin.
Recall that the space is throughout the whole master thesis $\R^d$ with $d\in\{2,3\}$, thus not the phase space.

\section{The heuristic and the definition}

In words we can describe this design in this way:
\begin{enumerate}
\item There is a fixed number $N$ of amoebas, each subjected to its own Brownian motion with constant diffusion coefficient $\sigma_\amba$
\item Each amoeba $\amba^p$ attracted to the cAMP molecules with a potential $\potential$ which has properties [[PROPERTIES MODELLED ON THE GAUSSIAN]]
\item Each amoeba $\amba^p$ spawns cAMP molecules $\camp^{p,g}$ (p=parent, g=generation) with a Poisson process $\hmactive^p$ of parameter $\lambda$ indipendent of $p$
\item Each cAMP molecule is born at the position of its parent and is subjected from that time on to its own Brownian motion with constant diffusion coefficient $\sigma_\camp$
\item All the Brownian motions for each particle are indipendent from each others
\end{enumerate}

Let us see all of this in equations. Let $N\in\N$ be the number of amoebas, and fix $N$ indipendent Poisson processes $(\hmactive^p)_{i=1}^N$ of parameter $\lambda$.
Define for each parent $p \in \{1,\ldots,N\}$ and each generation $g \in \N$, the stopping time

\begin{equation}
\btime^{p,g} := \inf \{ t : \hmactive^p_t \geq g \}
\end{equation}

\ie the $\Gma_{g, \lambda}$ distributed \emph{spawning} or \emph{birth time} of the $\camp^{p,g}$ cAMP molecule.

Consider a countable collection of indipendent Brownian motions 

\begin{equation}
\{B^{A,p}, B^{C,p,g}\}_{1\leq p\leq N,g\in\N} =:\{B^p, B^{p,g}\}_{p,g}
\end{equation}

\ie $N$ of them for the amoebas and a countable amount for the cAMP molecules.
Now we need an important passage to accurately define the Brownian movement of the cAMP particles.
In fact each of these molucules moves only after spawning, and thus such movement must be calibrated for starting after this moment, and not before.

\begin{definition}
Let $T$ be a stopping time and $B$ a Brownian motion.
Consider the process
\begin{equation}
B_t^{(T)} := B_{T+t} - B_T
\end{equation}
which is a \Bm (see for example \citep[Theorem 2.20]{le-gall}).
Then the \emph{Quasi Brownian Motion} $\quasibm_t^{B,T}$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\quasibm_t^{B,T} := "B_{t-T}^{(T)}\1_{t-T\geq 0}"
=
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
& 0 
& t<T
\\
& B_{t-T}^{(T)} = B_{t} - B_{T}
& t \geq T
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}\label{ch2:def:quasi-bm}

\end{figure}

Then in our case, since $\forall p,g$ a unique \Bm and birth time is identified, we can write

\begin{equation}
\quasibm_t^{p,g} := \quasibm_t^{B^{p,g}, T^{p,g}}
= "B_{t-\btime^{p,g}}^{p,g,(\btime^{p,g})}\1_{t-\btime^{p,g}\geq 0}"
\end{equation}

which is lighter on notation.
With these independent processes we can now finally write down the model as a set of Poisson processes and stochastic differential equations:

\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
(\hmactive^p_t)_{p=1}^N 
&\text{ iid and } \sim \Pois_{\lambda t}
&
\\
\btime^{p,g} 
&:= \inf \{ t : \hmactive^p_t \geq g \}
&
\\
\amba_t^p 
&= \amba_0^p 
+ \int_0^t \frac{1}{N} \sum _{q = 1}^{N} \sum_{g = 1}^{\hmactive_s^q} \attraction(\amba_s^p - \camp_s^{q,g}) d s
+ 
\overbrace{\sigma^A B_t^p}^{\int_0^t \sigma_A d B_s^p}
&
\\
\camp_t^{p,g} 
&\qquad\text{does not exist} 
&\text{if} \quad t < \btime^{p,g}
\\
\camp_t^{p,g}
&= \amba_{\btime^{p,g}}^p + \underbrace{\sigma_C \quasibm_t^{p,g}}_{\int_0^t \sigma_C Q_s^{p,g}} 
&\text{if} \quad t \geq \btime^{p,g}
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}\label{ch2:model}

\paragraph{Observations} One can immediately notice that not even the dimension of the system \ref{ch2:model} is deterministic: the particles $\camp_t^{p,g}$ are spawning at the stochastic time $\btime^{p,g}$, increasing at each of these instants the number of particles involved (\ie the system is piecewise defined in time, according to stochastic times).
This, together with the fact that the Poisson processes $\hmactive^p$ cannot be represented with a SDE and that the $Q_t^{p,g}$ are not proper Brownian motions, makes this system formally not a system of SDEs, even though the equations involved are indeed differential and stochastic.

\subsection{Alternative formulation}

We can build a mathematically handier version of the previous model.
Since the Poisson processes $M^p$s determining the spawning times of particles are independent for each amoeba, we have that

\begin{equation}
\hmactive_t := \sum_{p=1}^N \hmactive_t^p
\end{equation}

is $\Pois(N t \lambda)$-distributed. Therefore the correspondent birth times

\begin{equation}
\btime_m := \inf \{ t : \hmactive_t \geq m\}
\end{equation}

are $\Gma(m, N\lambda)$-distributed.
Consider these as spawning times and re-indicise the cAMP particles consequently (\ie $\camp^m$ is the $m$-th spawned molecule, born at time $\btime_m$).
Then which amoeba $\amba^p$ is spawning $\camp^m$?

\begin{definition}
Define the random function $\Parentof : \N \longrightarrow \{1, \ldots, N\}$ as
\begin{equation}
\Parentof(m) := \{ p\; : \; A^p \text{ is the parent of } \camp^m \}
\end{equation}
This function is called \emph{parenting function}, and for each $m\in\N$ the random variable $\Parentof(m)$ is uniformly distirbuted on the amoebas $\{1, \ldots, N\}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proof}
\begin{align*}
\Prb (\Parentof(m) = p) 
&= \Prb (\exists g \in \N. \btime_m = \btime^{p,g} )
\\
&= \sum_{g=1}^{\infty} \Prb (\btime_m = \btime^{p,g})
\\
&= \sum_{g=1}^{\infty} \Prb (\hmactive_{\btime^{p,g}} = m)
\\
&= \sum_{g=1}^{\infty} \Prb (\hmactive_{\btime^{p,g}}^p = g, \sum_{k\neq p} \hmactive_{\btime^{p,g}}^k = m - g)
\\
&= \sum_{g=1}^{\infty} \Prb (g = g, \sum_{k\neq p} \hmactive_{\btime^{p,g}}^k = m - g)
\\
&= \sum_{g=1}^{\infty} \int \Prb( \sum_{k\neq p} \hmactive_{\btime^{p,g}}^k = m - g) 
	\big| \btime^{p,g} = s) d\Prb(\btime^{p,g} = s)
\\
&= \sum_{g=1}^{\infty} \int \Pois_{(N-1)s\lambda}(\{ m-g\})\1_{m-g \geq 0} 
	\underbrace{d\Prb(\btime^{p,g} = s)}_{:=\Zhi(p,g,s) ds}
\end{align*}

And since $\Zhi(p,g,s)ds=d\Gma_{g,\lambda} (s)$, it is independent from $p$, we have that $\Prb (\Parentof(m) = p) = const(m)$ and thus uniformly distributed on $\{1,\ldots,N\}$.
\end{proof}

Then we can reformulate the model \ref{ch2:model} as:

\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
(\hmactive^p_t)_{p=1}^N 
&\text{ iid and } \sim \Pois_{\lambda t}
,\: \hmactive_t := \sum_{p=1}^N \hmactive_t^p
&
\\
\btime_m
&:= \inf \{ t : \hmactive_t \geq m \}
&
\\
\amba_t^p 
&= \amba_0^p 
+ \int_0^t \frac{1}{N} \sum _{m = 1}^{\hmactive_s} \attraction(\amba_s^p - \camp_s^m) d s
+ 
\overbrace{\sigma^A B_t^p}^{\int_0^t \sigma_A d B_s^p}
&
\\
\camp_t^m 
&\qquad\text{does not exist} 
&if \quad t < \btime^m
\\
\camp_t^m
&= \amba_{\btime^m}^{\Parentof(m)} + \underbrace{\sigma_C \quasibm_t^m}_{\int_0^t \sigma_C Q_s^m}
&if \quad t \geq \btime^m
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}\label{ch2:model-reformulation}

This makes the equations simpler and let us arrange the spawning time chronologically, a feature which is important to show that the system has a solution.

\section{Existence and uniqueness of solutions}

The best chance we have to prove existence and uniqueness of solutions for the defined system is to find a way to see it (or parts of it) as proper SDEs and use the theorems of such framework to reach the goal.

\subsection{The SDEs framework}\label{ch2:section:sde-framework}

An SDE is an expression of the form:

\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
d X_t &= \drift(t, X_t) dt + \diffusion(t, X_t) d B_t	\\
X_0 &\sim \rho_0
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}\label{ch2:eq:general-sde}

which is a more conceptual notation to formally mean

\begin{equation}
X_t = \rho_0 + \int_0^t \drift(t, X_t) \,dt + \int_0^t \diffusion(t, X_t) \,d B_t
\end{equation}\label{ch2:eq:general-sde-integral-form}

where $\drift \in \R^n$, $\diffusion \in \R^{n \times m}$ are given vectorial and matricial functions respectively (the given initial distribution $\rho_0$ is optional).
They are called \emph{drift} and \emph{diffusion} (or \emph{volatility}) respectively.
We sign such SDE as a $E(\drift,\diffusion)$.
\begin{definition}
A solution for $E(\drift,\diffusion)$ consists of
\begin{itemize}
\item a complete filtered space $(\Omega, \Flt, (\Flt_t)_t, \Prb) $
\item an $m$-dimensional $(\Flt_t)_t$-Brownian motion $B = (B^1, \ldots, B^m)$
\item an $(\Flt_t)_t$-adapted $n$-dimensional process $X_t$ with $\Cspace^0$ sample paths
\end{itemize}
such that they satisfy the equation $E(\drift,\diffusion)$.
If furthermore $X_0 = x$ we say that $X$ solves $E_x(\drift,\diffusion)$.
\end{definition}

To ensure existence (strong existence, meaning that we can find a solution on each filtered probability space endowed with a Brownian motion) and uniqueness (path uniqueness, meaning that having two solutions $X_t, Y_t$ with the same initial conditions, they are indistinguishable, \ie $\PrbOf{ \forall	t. X_t = Y_t }=1 $) of solutions for $E(\drift, \diffusion)$ we need to require conditions on $\drift, \diffusion$. There are two main results:
\begin{itemize}
\item \autocite[Thm 8.3]{le-gall}: If the two coefficients are Lipschitz (eventually only locally) in space and continuous in time, then there is existence and uniqueness of solutions for $E(\drift, \diffusion)$:
\begin{equation*}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
\forall t .( |\drift(t, x)-\drift(t,y)| &< L_\drift |x-y| &\text{ and } |\diffusion(t, x)-\diffusion(t,y)| &< L_\diffusion |x-y|) \\
\drift &\in \Cspace^0 &\text{ and }\diffusion &\in \Cspace^0
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation*}
\item \autocite[Thm 9.4]{baldi}: If the two coefficients are Lipschitz in space (as before) and have sublinear growth in space, then there is existence and uniqueness of solutions for $E(\drift, \diffusion)$:
\begin{equation*}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
&\forall t .(|\drift(t, x)-\drift(t,y)| < L_\drift |x-y| 
&\:\text{ and }\:
&|\diffusion(t, x)-\diffusion(t,y)| < L_\diffusion |x-y|) 
\\
&\forall t .|\drift(t, x)| < M_b(1 + |x|) 
&\:\text{ and }\:
&|\diffusion(t, x)| < M_\diffusion (1 + |x|)
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation*}\label{ch2:cond:E-and-U-lipsch+subgrowth}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Reducing the model to the SDE framework}

\subsubsection{Heuristic}

The system \ref{ch2:model} is not a proper SDE because of technical oddities:
\begin{itemize}
\item An hypothetical vector $\sol_t := (\amba_t^1,\ldots, \amba_t^N, \camp_t^{p,g} \text{ already spawned at time }t)$ would have a dimension changing (growing, in particular) as the time flows.
And it grows up to infinity.
\item Not only it would be changing with the time, but it would be a \emph{stochastic} dimension.
\item If we were to write the equations for $\sol$ in a similar way to the one in $\ref{ch2:eq:general-sde}$ we would have a drift term $\drift = \drift(t, \sol_t, (\hmactive_t^p)_p)$ depending also on the Poisson-distributed processes $(\hmactive_t^p)_p$, making it "too much stochastical" to be treated as in \ref{ch2:eq:general-sde}.
\end{itemize}

All these oddities originate from the fact that our system depends on $N$ sequences $(\btime^{p,g})_{g}$ of \emph{random} birth times (or equivalently from the Poisson processes generating them, \ie $(\hmactive_t^p)_t$).
We can notice that, while the movement of amoebas $\amba^p$ and cAMP molecules $\camp^{p,g}$ depends on them, such sequences do not depend on $\amba^p, \camp^{p,g}$.
Then heuristically we can imagine to fix these sequences to an arbitrary value $(\tau^{p,g})_{p,g} = (\btime^{p,g})_{p,g}$ (obtaining an equivalent $(\mu_t^p)_{p,t}=(\hmactive_t^p)_{t,p}$), and inquire if this system has a solution for it:

\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
\amba_t^p 
&= \amba_0^p 
+ \int_0^t 
	\overbrace{
		\frac{1}{N} \sum _{p = 1}^{N} \sum_{g = 1}^{\mu_s^p} \attraction(\amba_s^p - \camp_s^{p,g})
	}^{=:\drift_p(s, \sol_s, (\mu_s^p)_p)=:\drift_p(s, \sol_s)} 
d s
+ 
\overbrace{\sigma^A}^{=:\diffusion_p(s,\sol_s)} B_t^p
&
\\
\camp_t^{p,g} 
&\qquad\text{does not exist} 
&if \quad t < \tau^{p,g}
\\
\camp_t^{p,g}
&= \amba_{\tau^{p,g}}^p + \underbrace{\sigma_C}_{=:\diffusion_{p,g}(s,\sol_s)} \quasibm_t^{p,g}
&if \quad t \geq \tau^{p,g}
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}\label{ch2:system:heuristic-fixed-times}

This solves the problem because $(\tau^{p,g})_{p,g}$ is deterministic.
Since the value is arbitrary, if we find a solution $(\sol, B)_{(\tau^{p,g})_{p,g}}$ for \ref{ch2:system:heuristic-fixed-times}, then we can assemble all these components into a global solution $(\sol, B)$.

This is formally explained by defining defining a solution for the system \ref{ch2:model} as a triple $((\hmactive^p)_p, \sol = (\amba, \camp), B)$ such that under the conditional probability measure

\begin{equation}
\overline{\Prb} : = \PrbCondTimes{\cdot} = \quad\PrbCond{\cdot}{(\hmactive^p)_{1\leq p\leq N, t\in\R_+}}
\end{equation}

the couple $(\sol,B)$ solves \ref{ch2:model}.
But we can notice that even in this way we need to operate piecewise in time to avoid a non-constant dimension (dictated by the number of particles, which is growing), \ie define the solution as one which on each interval where the dimension is constant, solves the system locally.

\subsubsection{Local solutions}

Consider the reformulation \ref{ch2:model-reformulation} and its birth times, which are chronologically ordered

\begin{equation}
\btime_0:=0 < \btime_1 < \btime_2 < \ldots < \btime_m < \ldots
\end{equation}

Define the correspondent intervals

\begin{equation}
I_0 := [0,\btime_1], I_1:=[\btime_1,\btime_2], \ldots , I_m:= [\btime_1,\btime_{m+1}],\ldots
\end{equation}

On each interval $I_m$ the system can be described as this:

\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
t &\in I_m = [\btime_m,\btime_{m+1}]
&
\\
\amba_t^p 
&= \amba_{\btime_m}^p 
+ \int_{\btime_m}^t 
	\frac{1}{N} \sum _{p = 1}^{N} \sum_{g = 1}^{\hmactive_s^p} \attraction(\amba_s^p - \camp_s^{p,g})
d s + \sigma^A B_t^p
&
\\
\camp_t^{p,g} 
&= \camp_{\btime^{p,g}}^{p,g} + \sigma_C \quasibm_t^{p,g}
&\text{if} \quad \btime^{p,g} < \btime_m
\\
\camp_t^{p,g}
&= \amba_{\btime^{p,g}}^p + \sigma_C \quasibm_t^{p,g}
&\text{when} \quad \btime^{p,g} = \btime_m
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}\label{ch2:system:local-model}

\begin{remark}
We need to observe that only in $I_m \smallsetminus \{\btime_{m+1}\}$ the number of particles (and thus the dimension) is constant, so why can we claim that the model \ref{ch2:model} is described efficiently by \ref{ch2:system:local-model} efficiently on $I_m$?
Since the $\{\btime_{m+1}\}$ has Lebesgue-measure $0$ the trajectory of the particles already spawned in $I_m \smallsetminus \{\btime_{m+1}\}$ is not influenced in $I_m$ by $\camp^{m+1}$.
Then we can ignore its existence to determine the trajectories of the other particles in $I_m$ and have thus a constant dimension on such interval.
\end{remark}

Now the only passage that we need in order to describe \ref{ch2:system:local-model} as a proper SDE is that of transforming the quasi-Brownian motions $\quasibm^{p,g}$ into proper Brownian motions.
This is easily done by shifting the system in time from $I_m$ to $I_m-\btime_m=[0, \btime_{m+1}-\btime_m]$, obtaining:

\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
t &\in I_m - \btime_m = [0, \btime_{m+1}-\btime_m]
&
\\
\amba_t^p 
&= \amba_{\btime_m}^p
	+ \int_0^t 
		\overbrace{
			\frac{1}{N} \sum _{p = 1}^{N} \sum_{g = 1}^{\hmactive_s^p} \attraction(\amba_s^p - \camp_s^{p,g})
		}^{\drift_p}
	d s
	+ 
	\overbrace{\sigma^A}^{\diffusion_p} 
	\overbrace{(B_{t + \btime_m}^p - B_{\btime_m}^p)}
		^{
			=:\overline{B}_t^p \text{ (\Bm in $t$)}
		}
&
\\
\camp_t^{p,g} 
&= \camp_{\btime^{p,g}}^{p,g} + \sigma_C \quasibm_{\btime_m + t}^{p,g} = \camp_{\btime^{p,g}}^{p,g} + \sigma_C
	\overbrace{(\quasibm_{t + \btime_m}^{p,g} - \quasibm_t^{p,g})}
	^{
		\scriptsize{
			\begin{gathered}
				\\
				=:\overline{B}_t^{p,g} \text{ (\Bm in $t$)}
				\\
				= (B_{t + \btime_m}^{p,g} - B_{\btime_m}^{p,g})
			\end{gathered}
		}	
	}
&\text{if} \quad \btime^{p,g} < \btime_m
\\
\camp_t^{p,g}
&= \amba_{\btime^{p,g}}^p + \sigma_C \quasibm_{\btime_m + t}^{p,g}
	= \amba_{\btime^{p,g}}^p + \underbrace{\sigma_C}_{\diffusion_{p,g}} 
		+ \underbrace{B_t^{p,g}}_{=:\overline{B}_t^{p,g}}
&\text{when} \quad \btime^{p,g} = \btime_m
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}\label{ch2:system:local-model-shifted-to-0}

Suppose that the initial conditions $\amba_{\btime_m}^p,\camp_{\btime_m}^{p,g}$ are given and that such equations in \ref{ch2:system:local-model-shifted-to-0} have a solution $({}^m Y_t, \overline{B}_t)$ on $I_m - \btime_m$.
Define then on $I_m$ the processes

\begin{align*}
{}^{m}\sol_t := {}^m Y_{t-\btime_m}
\qquad
& B_t^{\star} := \overline{B}_{t-\btime_m}^{\star}
\end{align*}

Then it is straightforward to verify that $({}^{m}\sol_t,B_t^{\star})$ fullfilles the equations on $I_m$, \ie \ref{ch2:system:local-model}, with the correspondent initial conditions.

\subsubsection{Global solutions}

\begin{definition}
A solution to \ref{ch2:model} is a triple $((\hmactive^p)_{1\leq p \leq N}, \sol = (\amba, \camp), B)$ such that:
\begin{itemize}
\item $(\hmactive^p)_{1\leq p\leq N}$ are Poisson processes of parameter $\lambda$ and do not depend on $(\sol, B)$
\item Considering the random measure $\PrbCondTimes{\cdot}{}$ and the interval $I_m := [\btime_m, \btime_{m+1}]$ we have that the couple $(\sol = (\amba, \camp), B)$ on $I_m \setminus \{ \btime_{m+1}\} = [\btime_m, \btime_{m+1})$ takes the form $({}^m\sol := (\amba, \camp^1, \ldots, \camp^m), {}^mB := (B^A, \quasibm^1, \ldots, \quasibm^m))$ and such couple $({}^m\sol, {}^mB)$ solves the local system \ref{ch2:system:local-model} on the whole interval $I_m$.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

In order for this to be a valid definition we should specify a global codomain for the processes $\sol, B$ and we should verify that $\PrbCondTimes{\cdot}{}$ is a \emph{regular} random probability measure, so that the SDEs considered remain valid.
This is hinted in the appendix at \ref{app:formal-def-solution}.

\begin{remark}
\begin{enumerate}
\item In the interval $I_m$ the number of particles is
\begin{equation}
\NumParticles_t := N + \sum_{p=1}^N \hmactive_t^p = \overbrace{N}^{\# \text{amoebas}} + \overbrace{\hmactive_t}^{\# \text{cAMP}}
\end{equation}
thus the dimension of the system is $\NumParticles_t \cdot d$, where $d$ is the dimension in which we are considering the particles ($2$ or $3$).
\label{ch2:dimension-system}
\item Since by definition the particle $\camp^{m+1}$ spawns on the point $\amba_{\btime_{m+1}}^p$ for some $p$, the solution ${}^{m+1}\sol$ on the interval $I_{m+1}$ is glued with continuouty with the solution ${}^{m}\sol$ on $I_{m}$ for all the already existing particles.
Furthermore, this continuouty property remains true even regarding the spawning position of $\camp^{m+1}$, meaning that such particle spawns on $\amba_{\btime_{m+1}}^{\Parentof(m+1)}$.
This can be visualised as the property that at each birth time $\btime_m$ the path of one of the amoebas bifurcates (see \ref{ch2:fig:path-bifurcation}).
\label{ch2:system-continuous-glueing}
\begin{figure}[h]
\begin{tikzpicture}
\end{tikzpicture}
\caption{[[[HERE DRAW TIKZ FIGURE WITH ALL THE BIFURCATIONS AT THE RANDOM TIMES]]]}
\end{figure}
\label{ch2:fig:path-bifurcation}
\item Glueing the local solutions we might ask if for each particle its diffusion is \emph{globally} still a \Bm, or a quasi-\Bm (under the measure $\PrbCondTimes{}{}$).
The question depends essentially on this: \invquest given two indipendent \Bm $B^1$ and $B^2$ and a stopping time $T$, is it true that the process
\begin{equation*}
B^{T,1,2}_t := B_{t \wedge T} ^1 + \1_{t\geq T} (B_t^2 - B_T^2)
\end{equation*}
is also Brownian motion?
It is easy to verify that the answer is affirmative (use \eg \autocite[Proposition 2.3]{le-gall} and discard the non-continuous sample-paths), and that thus for each particle we have a global \Bm or quasi-\Bm as a diffusion, \ie the expressions $B^p$ and $\quasibm^{p,g}$ still make sense globally.
\end{enumerate}
\end{remark}



Let us sign the coordinates of space as 

\begin{align*}
x &:= (x^1, \ldots, x^N, x^{1,1}, x^{1,2}, \ldots, x^{p,1},\ldots, x^{p,g},x^{p,g+1},\ldots, x^{N,1}, \ldots)
\\
	&\ = (a^1, \ldots, a^N, c^{1,1}, \ldots, c^{p,g}, \ldots) 
\end{align*}

Then we can notice that all the systems considered (the model \ref{ch2:model}, its local version \ref{ch2:system:local-model} and its local shifted-in-time version \ref{ch2:system:local-model-shifted-to-0}) share the same expression for the functions which would represent the drift and the diffusion in an SDE

\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
\drift_k(t,x, (\hmactive^k_t)_k) &:= \drift_k(t,x) := \frac{1}{N} \sum _{p = 1}^{N} \sum_{g = 1}^{\hmactive_t^p} \attraction(x^k - x^{p,g})
	&& 1\leq k \leq N
\\
\drift_{p,g}(t,x) &:= 0
	&& \text{if } \exists (p,g)
\\
\diffusion_p(t,x) &:= \sigma_A
	&& 1\leq p \leq N
\\
\diffusion_{p,g}(t,x) &:= \sigma_C
	&& \text{if } \exists (p,g)
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}\label{ch2:def:drift-&-diffusion}

At first sight it would seem that these expressions would require to be manipulated in order to become the drift and the diffusion of \ref{ch2:system:local-model-shifted-to-0}, because of the time translation of $\btime_m$.
But the time dependence is given only by the random value $\hmactive_s^p$, which is fixed under $\PrbCondTimes{\cdot}{}$, giving us thus the exact same definition for the drift and diffusion throughout all systems.

\begin{remark}\label{ch2:rmk:notation-indices-drift}
	Notice that $\drift_p(t,x),\: \drift_{p,g}(t,x)\:\in \R^d$.
	To avoid ambiguity between such $d$-dimensional components of the drift and its $1$ dimensional ones, we sign as 
	\begin{equation*}
		\drift_{p;i}(t,x),\: \drift_{p,g;i}(t,x)
	\end{equation*}
	the $i$-th coordinate of the $d$-dimensional component for the functions $\drift_p(t,x),\: \drift_{p,g}(t,x)\:\in \R^d$ respectively.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Ensure the existence and uniqueness for the model}

Now that we have reduced our model to a collection of conditional, piecewise-in-time defined SDE, it is enough to find a solution inductively for each $I_m$ under $\PrbCondTimes{\cdot}{}$, \ie a unique solution for \ref{ch2:system:local-model-shifted-to-0} under such random probability measure.
Since the case $m=1$ has essentialy the same expression for the drift and diffusion as the arbitrary case $m$, we consider only the latter one.

In order to prove local existence and uniqueness we must watch at the drift and diffusion of the system (\ref{ch2:def:drift-&-diffusion}).
The diffusion coefficients are constant, and thus respect all the conditions required in \ref{ch2:cond:E-and-U-lipsch+subgrowth}.
The same is true for the drift of the cAMP.
This is not the case for the drift in the case of amoebas though: since $\hmactive^p_t$ are \cadlag, then also the coordinate functions $\drift_p(t, x)$ are \cadlag, \ie not $\Cspace^0$ in time.
We need thus to use the result \ref{ch2:cond:E-and-U-lipsch+subgrowth}.

\begin{lemma}
The drift function $\drift(t, x, (\hmactive^p)_p)$ defined coordinate-wise in \ref{ch2:def:drift-&-diffusion} as
\begin{equation*}
\drift(t,x) := \drift(t,x, (\hmactive^p_t)_t) := 
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
&\drift_k(t,x) := \frac{1}{N} \sum _{p = 1}^{N} \sum_{g = 1}^{\hmactive_t^p} \attraction(x^k - x^{p,g})
\\
&\drift_{p,g}(t,x) := 0
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation*}
is such that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item it is Lipschitz continuous in space with fixed time with Lipschitz constant
\begin{equation*}
L_N := \frac{2l\hmactive_t}{N} = \frac{2l}{N} \sum_{k=1}^N \hmactive_t^p
\end{equation*}
where $l$ is the Lipschitz constant of the attraction $\attraction$.
\ie
\begin{equation}
\forall t \in \R_+.\ || \drift(t,x) - \drift(t,y)||_\infty \leq L_N || x-y||_\infty
\end{equation}
\item It is bounded, hence in particular it grows sublinearly in space:
\begin{equation*}
\exists M > 0.\,\forall t \in \R_+.\ |\drift(t,x) - \drift(t,y)| \leq M (1+|x|)
\end{equation*}
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}\label{ch2:lemma:lipsch-of-drift}

\begin{remark}
Since we work in a finite Banach space all norms are equivalent, thus for these properties we could choose any of them.
Still, the Lipschitz constant of $\drift$ with the infinity norm will play a more relevant role later, so for that instance we decided to specify it.
\end{remark}

\begin{proof}
Fix $t,x,y$. Then

\begin{equation*}
\begin{aligned}
	|| \drift(t, x) - \drift(t, y) ||_\infty
	&= \max_{k\in\{1,\ldots,N\}} || \drift_k(t, x) - \drift_k(t, y) ||_\infty
	\\
	&= \max_{k\in\{1,\ldots,N\}} ||
			\frac{1}{N} \sum_{p=1}^{N} \sum_{g = 1}^{\hmactive_t^p} \attraction(x^k - x^{p,g}) 
			- \frac{1}{N} \sum_{p=1}^{N} \sum _{g = 1} ^{\hmactive_t^p} \attraction(y^k - y^{p,g}) 
		||_\infty
	\\
	& \leq \frac{1}{N} \max_{k\in\{1,\ldots,N\}}
			\sum_{p=1}^{N} \sum_{g = 1}^{\hmactive_t^p}
			\underbrace{|| \attraction(x^k - x^{p,g}) - \attraction(y^k - y^{p,g}) ||_\infty}
			_{\leq l ||(x^k - x^{p,g}) - (y^k - y^{p,g})||_\infty }
	\\
	&\qquad \text{(Since $\attraction$ has Lipschitz constant $l$)}
	\\
	& \leq \frac{l}{N} \max_{k\in\{1,\ldots,N\}}
		\sum_{p=1}^{N} \sum_{g = 1}^{\hmactive_t^p}
			\big(
			||x^k - y^k ||_\infty + || x^{p,g} - y^{p,g}||_\infty
			\big)
	\\
	& = \frac{l}{N} \sum_{p=1}^{N} \sum_{g = 1}^{\hmactive_t^p}
	\big(
		\max_{k\in\{1,\ldots,N\}} ||x^k - y^k||_\infty + || x^{p,g} - y^{p,g}||_\infty
	\big)
	\\
	& \leq \frac{l}{N} \sum_{p=1}^{N} \sum_{g = 1}^{\hmactive_t^p}
	\big(
		||x - y ||_\infty + || x - y||_\infty
	\big) \ = \frac{2l}{N} \sum_{p=1}^{N} \hmactive_t^p ||x - y ||_\infty
\end{aligned}
\end{equation*}

And thus the Lipschitzianity has been proved.
The sublinearity is easily achieved because the potential is bounded, and thus $\drift$ is bounded.
For example, using the $\infty$-norm we have:
\begin{equation*}
\begin{aligned}
|| \drift(t, x)||_\infty
	& \leq \frac{l}{N} \max_{k\in\{1,\ldots,N\}}
		\sum_{p=1}^{N} \sum_{g = 1}^{\hmactive_t^p}
		\underbrace{|| \attraction(x^k - x^{p,g})||_\infty}
			_{\leq \cdot || \attraction||_\infty}
	\\
	& \leq \frac{l \sum_{p=1}^{N} \hmactive_t^p ||\attraction||_\infty}{N}
	\\
	& = \frac{L_N}{2} ||\attraction||_\infty \leq \frac{L_N}{2} ||\attraction||_\infty (1 + ||x||_\infty)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
\end{proof}

Therefore the conditions for the pathwise uniqueness and the strong existence of solutions of \ref{ch2:cond:E-and-U-lipsch+subgrowth} are fullfilled, and thus the system \ref{ch2:system:local-model} has the properties of pathwise uniqueness and strong existence of solutions, \ie under $\PrbCondTimes{\cdot}{}$ on each interval $I_m = [\btime_m, \btime_{m+1}]$ there is a unique solution on each filtered probability space endowed with a \Bm .
To be precise, the solution is unique for each initial condition $x_m$ for the local solution ${}^m \sol$, but since $\forall m \geq 2$ all the initial conditions are determined by the previous local solution ${}^{m+1}\sol$, then there is a global solution to \ref{ch2:model} which is unique for each initial condition $(a_1, \ldots, a_N)$.
