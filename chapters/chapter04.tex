\section{What are effective equations?}

Effective equations are macroscopic description of system of many particles interacting with each others.
The idea is that part of the very complex behaviour of many bodies interacting with each others can be averaged out on a bigger scale, leaving thus only the essential part of the interactions.
We start from a cinematic $\sol_t = (\amba_t, \camp_t) \in (\R^d)^{\NumParticles_t}$, the solution to one of the models, with initial condition

\begin{equation}
\amba_0 \sim \adens_0 \otimes \ldots \otimes \adens_0
\end{equation}

for some distribution $\adens_0 \in \Lpspace^1 \cap \Lpspace^\infty$.
We search for a system of PDEs

\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
\partial_t \adens(t, x) &= \ldots
\\
\partial_t \cdens(t, x) &= \ldots
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}

such that the its solutions $\adens, \cdens: \text{time} \times \text{space} \longrightarrow \R_+ $ (under the same initial condition,\ie $\adens(0,\cdot)=\adens_0$) represent faithfully (in some sense yet to be explained) respectively the density of the amoebas and of the cAMP molecules when the number of amoebas $N$ tends to infinity.

In order to formalise the definition, we must understand that the number of initial amoebas $N$ is here relevant, therefore we sign

\begin{equation}
\sol_t = \sol_t^N = ( \sol_t^{N,k})_k \in (\R^d )^{\NumParticles_t} = (\R^d)^{\NumParticles_{N,t}}
\end{equation}

as the solution to the model with $N$ amoebas.
Consider now $\Psi_t^{\amba,N}:=\law(\amba_t), \Psi_t^{\camp,N}:=\law(\camp_t)$, \ie the law of the amoeba's and the cAMP's parts of the solution $\sol_t^N$.
Consider its $(k)$-th marginals

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
{}^{(k)}\Psi_t^{\amba,N}
&:= \int_{\R^{d(N-k)}} \Psi_t^{\amba,N} \, dx_{k+1} \ldots dx_N
\\
{}^{(k)}\Psi_t^{\camp,N}
&:= \int_{\R^{d(\NumParticles-k)}} \Psi_t^{\camp,N} \, dx_{k+1} \ldots dx_{\NumParticles}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Then we can formalise the notion of faithful representation, at least for the amoebas' part, as the condition that for each $t\geq 0, k \geq 1$ we have that ${}^{(k)}\Psi_t^{\amba,N}$ converge weakly to $\otimes_{p=1}^{N}\adens_t$ (for $N$ going to $\infty$), \ie in symbols

\begin{equation}
\forall t\geq 0, k \geq 1 \quad
{}^{(k)}\Psi_t^{\amba,N} \rightharpoonup \bigotimes_{p=1}^{k}\adens_t
\end{equation}

It will be more comfortable to operate in a bounded time-interval $[0, T]$ for an arbitrary $T > 0$, and thus the equivalent condition will become

\begin{equation}
\forall 0\leq t\leq T, k \geq 1 \quad
{}^{(k)}\Psi_t^{\amba,N} \rightharpoonup \bigotimes_{p=1}^{k}\adens_t
\end{equation}\label{ch4:marginals-convergence}

For the cAMP's part this notion is more complicated, because it spawns at \emph{stochastic} times which, as $N$ grows, do grow in number as well, till they are dense in $[0,T]$.
We will better approach it later, where it will be also clearer what we are proving.
For now we observe, that even for the amoebas' part, working directly with the law of $\amba_t$ is cumbersome, and thus we need an alternative way to reach the condition \ref{ch4:marginals-convergence}.

\subsection{A metric for the weak-convergence of probability measures}

Now we need a concept of \emph{distance} between measures: the \emph{$p$-Wasserstein distance}, also known as \emph{$p$-Kantorovich–Rubinstein} or \emph{Earth-mover distance} (EMD) because of the heuristic that we will see in \ref{ch4:fig:example-coupling}.
First we need the concept of \emph{coupling} of measures.

\begin{definition}
Consider the space of Borel probability measures on $\R^\Ch$
\begin{equation}
\PrbMeas := \PrbMeas (\R^\Ch) := 
	\{\mu :
		\mu \text{ is a \emph{Borel} prob. meas. on } \R^\Ch
	\}
\end{equation}
and let $\mu, \nu \in \PrbMeas$. A \emph{coupling of $\mu$ and $\nu$} is a measure $\gamma \in \PrbMeas (\R^\Ch \times \R^\Ch)$ such that for all Borel-measurable and bounded functions $\phi$ we have
\begin{equation}
\int_{\R^\Ch \times \R^\Ch} \phi(x) \,d\gamma(x,y) = \int_{\R^\Ch} \phi(x) \,d\mu(x) 
\quad \text{ and } \quad
\int_{\R^\Ch \times \R^\Ch} \phi(y) \,d\gamma(x,y) = \int_{\R^\Ch} \phi(y) \,d\nu(x) 
\end{equation}\label{ch4:def-coupling}
We sign as $\Gamma(\mu,\nu)$ the set of couplings of $\mu$ and $\nu$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
\begin{enumerate}
\item The set $\Gamma(\mu,\nu)$ is never empty because the product measure $\mu \otimes \nu$ is always in it.
\item By signing as $\pi_i(x_1, x_2):= x_i$ the projection $\pi_i: \R^\Ch \times \R^\Ch \longrightarrow \R^\Ch$, we can observe that the condition \ref{ch4:def-coupling} is equivalent to require that
\begin{equation}
(\pi_1 )_\star \gamma = \mu
\quad \text{ and } \quad
(\pi_2)_\star \gamma = \nu
\end{equation}
where $\big((\pi_i)_\star \gamma \big)(E) := \gamma (\pi_i^{-1} (E))$ is the pushforward of $\gamma$ by $\pi_i$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{remark}

A way to visualise a coupling is as a transformation of $\mu$ into $\nu$.
Assume, for the sake of simplicity, that $\mu,\nu$ are measures on $\R$ and are absolute continuous with respect to the Lebesgue measure $\lebsge$.
Consider the densities $\frac{d\mu}{d\lebsge}(x), \frac{d\nu}{d\lebsge}(x)$ and think of their graphs as piles of earth (see \ref{ch4:fig:example-coupling}).

A plan to move earth in $\R$ can be thought as a function $\overline{\gamma}: \R \times \R \longrightarrow \R $ which with the value $\overline{\gamma}(x,y)=: \overline{\gamma}(x\rightarrow y)$ tells how much earth we should move from the point $x$ to the point $y$.
In order for this to be a plan which precisely transforms $\frac{d\mu}{d\lebsge}$ into $\frac{d\nu}{d\lebsge}$, it must respect two symmetrical conditions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item For each $x\in\R$ the total earth (mass of) moved from it by the plan must be exactly equal to the earth present in $x$ at the beginning, namely:
\begin{equation}
\int \overline{\gamma}(x,y) \,dy = \frac{d\mu}{d\lebsge}(x)
\end{equation}
\item For each $y\in\R$ the earth (the mass) moved to it must be exactly equal to the earth present in $y$ at the end, namely:
\begin{equation}
\int \overline{\gamma}(x,y) \,dx = \frac{d\nu}{d\lebsge}(y)
\end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

This is equivalent to \ref{ch4:def-coupling}, with $\overline{\gamma}$ playing the role of the density of the coupling $\gamma$.
With this example in mind we could ask ourselves which is the best among such transformations of $\mu$ into $\nu$.
To do that we need to associate to each coupling $\gamma \in \Gamma(\mu,nu)$ a value, which indicates how "good" a plan $\gamma$ might be.
If we have a notion of distance $dist$ on our space $\R^\Ch$, then a plan can be evaluated with how much net distance we cover to transport all the earth around, \ie with the expression

\begin{equation}
\int dist(x,y) \,d\gamma(x,y)
\end{equation}

In this way we can say that two measures $\mu,\nu$ are near to each others if we can find a coupling $\gamma \in \Gamma(\mu,\nu)$ such that its evaluation is very low.
With this idea in mind, choosing the distance induced by the normalised $1$-norm on $\R^\Ch$ (meaning $\frac{|\cdot|_1}{\Ch}$), one reaches the definition of the $1$-Wassertein distance (with respect to the normalised $1$-norm).

\begin{definition}
Consider the space of Borel probability measures with finite $1$-moments on $\R^\Ch$
\begin{equation}
\PrbMeas_1 := \PrbMeas_1(\R^\Ch) := 
	\{\mu \in \PrbMeas :
		\int |x| d \mu (x) < \infty
	\} \subseteq \PrbMeas
\end{equation}
Then the $1$-Wasserstein distance between two measures $\mu,\nu \in \PrbMeas_p$ is defined as
\begin{equation}
W_1(\mu,\nu):= \inf_{\gamma \in \Gamma(\mu,\nu)}
	\int_{\R^\Ch \times \R^\Ch} \frac{1}{\Ch}\sum_{i=1}^{\Ch} |x_i-y_i| d\gamma(x,y)
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

To see the proof that $W_1$ is a distance take a look at the proof after \autocite[Example 6.3]{cedric-villani-opt-trans}.
The reason why we cover this concept is this result (from \autocite[Corollary 6.13]{cedric-villani-opt-trans}):

\begin{theorem}
If $(X,d)$ is a Polish space, and $\overline{d}$ is a bounded distance inducing the same topology on $X$ as $d$, then the convergence of measures in the $1$-Wasserstein sense for $\overline{d}$ is equivalent to the usual weak convergence of probability measures in $\PrbMeas_1(X)$.
\end{theorem}

Therefore we can obtain the convergence of the marginals (condition \ref{ch4:marginals-convergence}) of the amoebas' part if the subsequent condition is fullfilled:

\begin{equation}
\sup_{0\leq t\leq T} \quad
W_1 \big(
	{}^{(k)}\Psi_t^{\amba,N}, \bigotimes_{p=1}^{k}\adens_t
 \big) 
 \xrightarrow {N \rightarrow \infty} 0
\end{equation}

or even stronger:

\begin{equation}
\sup_{0\leq t\leq T} \quad
W_1 \big(
	\Psi_t^{\amba,N}, \bigotimes_{p=1}^{N}\adens_t
 \big) 
 \xrightarrow {N \rightarrow \infty} 0
\end{equation}\label{ch4:w-1-convergence-condition-amoebas}

\subsection{Mean-field particles and how to use them}

Starting from the effective equations solution $\adens_t$ we can associate to it, under some conditions, a stochastic process $\mfamba_t$ such that $\law(\mfamba_t) = \adens(t,\cdot)$.
In particular let us assume that the effective equation for $\adens_t$ takes the form

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\partial_t \adens(t,x) 
&=  - \sum_{i=1}^{d} \partial_{x_i}[ \mfdrift^i(t,x) \adens(t,x)] 
+ D_A \bigtriangleup \adens(t,x)
\\
D_A
&:= \frac{1}{2} \sum_{k=1}^{d} \sigma_A^2 = \frac{d\sigma_A^2}{2}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}\label{ch4:eq:shape-effective-eqs-for-mf-particles}

for some function $\mfdrift$ which respects the conditions that a drift $\drift$ of an SDE must respect in \ref{ch3:sde-framework} to ensure existence and uniqueness of solution.
Then we know that there is a unique solution in $[0,T]$ for the SDE

\begin{equation}
\mfamba_t
= \mfamba_0 + \int_0^t \mfdrift(s,\mfamba_s) ds + \sigma_A dB_t
\end{equation}

Furthermore, by \autocite[Theorem 8.1.1]{oksendal}, we know that, if $\adens(t,x)$ is a solution to \ref{ch4:eq:shape-effective-eqs-for-mf-particles}, then $\law(\mfamba_t) = \adens(t,x)$, \ie $\adens(t, \cdot)$ is the \emph{Kolmogorov forward equation} for latter SDE.

Now we can finally formulate a way to operate with the processes rather than with their laws.

\section{How make a smart guess for the effective equations and prove that it is the correct choice}

Instead of guessing the PDE, the idea is to guess the equivalent-mean field particles $\mfsol = (\mfamba^1, \ldots, \mfamba^N, \mfcamp^1, \ldots, \mfcamp^{\hmactive_t})$ solutions to a system of equations 

\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
d \mfamba^p_t &= \mfdrift^{N,p}(t,\mfamba_t^p) d t + \sigma_A d B_t^p &
\\
\mfcamp^{p,g}_t &= \text{ (some spawning position } \spawnpos_{\btime^{p,g}}^{p,g})) + \mfdrift^{N,p}(t,\mfcamp_t^p) + \sigma_C \quasibm_t^{p,g} &\text{from time }\btime^{p,g}
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}\label{ch4:eq:approximate-mean-field-equations}

where the drift terms $\mfdrift^{N,k}$ are calculated by taking the average of the original $\drift^p$s, \ie

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mfdrift^{N,k}(t, x) &:= \ECond{\drift^{N,k}(t, \mfsol_t)}{ \mfsol_t^k} (x)
\\
\mfdrift^{N,p,g}(t, x) &:= \ECond{\drift^{N,k}(t, \mfsol_t)}{ \mfsol_t^{p,g} } (x)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

and all the spawning positions $\spawnpos_{\btime^{p,g}}^{p,g}$ are independent from each others and from the $A^p$s.
Then one considers the correspondent Kolmogorov forward equation, and that is the effective equation searched for.
The idea behind this guess is that with a big number of amoebas and of cAMP molecules, their \emph{drift field} should resemble more and more the \emph{average drift field}, due to the fact that there are many long range interactions.
For this reason the choice of the name \emph{mean-field particles}.

\begin{remark}
\begin{enumerate}
\item We will also couple this system with the solution of \ref{ch2:model:deterministic-parenting}, meaning that we will take the same Poisson processes $(\hmactive^p)_p$ (thus birth times) and the same \Bm of such solution.
This is made in order to express the distance between the mean-field particles and the original ones in an easier way, namely for each coordinate

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
| \sol_t^k - \mfsol_t^k | 
&= | \int_0^t \drift^{N,k}(s,\sol_s) d s + \sigma B_t^k - \int_0^t \mfdrift^{N,k}(t,\mfsol_s^k) d s + \sigma B_t^k |
\\
&= | \int_0^t \drift^{N,k}(s,\sol_s)  - \mfdrift^{N,k}(t,\mfsol_s^k) d s|
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\item Notice that, while all the $\mfamba_t^p$ are \emph{iid}, the cAMP mean-fiel particles $\mfcamp_t^p$ are only \emph{independent} (\emph{not identically distributed}) and only \emph{under the conditioned probability} $ \PrbCondTimes{\cdot} $.
And it cannot be otherwise because of the coupling: indeed for each $\camp^{p,g}$ such quasi-\Bm $\quasibm^{p,g}_t$ starts to differ from $0$ only at $\btime^{p,g}$, and the molecule must necessary spawn at such time.
The disadvantage of not having identical distributions, is that crafting a Law of large numbers becomes more difficult.
\end{enumerate}
\end{remark}
