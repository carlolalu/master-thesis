In this section we want to address a bit more formally two closely related questions:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item What is the exact space of solutions for the solutions to the basic model? In the definition \ref{ch2:def:solution-model} did not specify the codomain of the \rv involved.
	\item How can we be sure that the conditioning $\Prb$ we are obtaining \emph{regular} conditional probability measures (\cite[Chapter 10.2,first definition]{dudley}), \ie that we obtain mathematical objects that behave as probability measures?
	This is an important question because for the definition \ref{ch2:def:solution-model} we need certain objects to be \Bm under $\PrbCondTimes{\cdot}{}$, thus we assume implicitly that $\PrbCondTimes{\cdot}{}$ is a probability measure.
\end{enumerate}

We will not give a complete answer to the second question, but we will give a direction to search for it, and we expect that with further work one might prove the necessary technicalities required to obtain the desired result.

\subsection{The Space of Solutions}\label{app:formal-def-solution}

We first need to check where the spawning Poisson processes $(\hmactive_t^p)_p$ are taking values for each fixed time $t$.
This is of course $(\N_0)^N$.
Now we define the space of configurations for the cAMP molecules $(\camp^{p,g})_g$ spawned by a single amoeba $\amba^p$.
Here the symbol "$\spadesuit$" means that a particle has not been spawned yet.

\begin{definition}
Define the space of the positions cAMP particles spawned by a single amoeba as:
\begin{equation*}
\confCamp(d):= (\{\spadesuit\} \sqcup \R^d)^\N
\end{equation*}
Define the space of configurations for all particles (the $N$ amoebas $\amba^p$ and the cAMP molecules $\camp^{p,g}$) as
\begin{equation*}
\Conf:=(\R^d)^N \times\big(\confCamp(d)\big)^N
\end{equation*}
For each configuration of particles $x\in \Conf$ sign its $d$-dimensional coordinates as 
\begin{equation*}
\begin{aligned}
x &= (x^1, \ldots, x^N, 
	&(x^{1,g})_{g\in\N}, \ldots, 
	&(x^{p,g})_{g\in\N}, \ldots, 
	&(x^{N,g})_{g\in\N},\ldots)
\\
&= (a^1, \ldots, a^N, 
	&c^{1,1}, c^{1,2}, \ldots, 
	&c^{p,1},\ldots, 
	&c^{N,1}, \ldots)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
where the absence of a coordinate in the list means that such coordinate is set to $\spadesuit$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
	One might want to define the configuration space of the cAMP as the more precise set
	\begin{align*}
		&\{
		x = (x_g)_{g\in\N}\
		:\
		\exists G\in\N_0.\
		\forall g < G.\
		x_g \in \R^d\
		\text{ and }\
		\forall g\geq G.\
		x_g =\spadesuit
		\}\subseteq
		\\
		&\subseteq\
		\bigoplus_{g=1}^\N (\{\spadesuit\} \sqcup \R^d)\
		:=
		\{
		x = (x_g)_{g\in\N}\
		:\
		\exists G\in\N_0.\
		\forall g\geq G.\
		x_g =\spadesuit
		\}
	\end{align*}
	But such space is very likely not Polish (the topic of the next section), thus we rely on the previous choice.
\end{remark}

And what about the Brownian motion associated to each particle? That is just a countable amount of independent $d$-dimensional \Bm.
With these definitions the solution of \ref{ch2:def:solution-model} can be seen as a \emph{random \cadlag} function 

\begin{equation*}
	((\hmactive^p)_p, \sol = (\amba, \camp), B) (\omega):\
	\R_+ \longrightarrow 
	\underbrace{
		(\N_0)^N
	}_{\text{Poisson processes}}
	\times
	\underbrace{
		(\R^d\times\confCamp(d))^N 
	}_{ \text{positions particles}}
		\times 
	\underbrace{
		(\R^d)^N \times (\confCamp(d))^N
	}_{\Bm}
\end{equation*}

or, in other words, the \rv $((\hmactive^p)_p, \sol, B)$ takes values in the set

\begin{equation}\label{app:def:space-of-solutions}
	\left\{
		f:\R_+ \longrightarrow 
			(\N_0
			\times
			\R^d\times\confCamp(d)
			\times 
			\R^d \times \confCamp(d))^N\
			:\
			f \text{ is \cadlag}
	\right\}
\end{equation}

It is not necessary to redefine the drift $\drift$ and the diffusion $\diffusion$, because they become relevant for the SDE framework only under the conditioning $\PrbCondTimes{}{}$. In any case, if we really wanted to see them in this framework, we would have for absent particle they have no mapping, so one could set them to  $\spadesuit$ for every cAMP molecule not yet spawned and obtain consistent mappings in the appropriate spaces.

\subsection{Can we Obtain Regular Probability Measures by Conditioning $\Prb$?}

For this chapter it is assumed the knowledge of \cite[Section 10.2]{dudley}.
The main instrument to verify that a measure is regular is \cite[Theorem 10.2.2]{dudley}, namely:

\begin{theorem}
	Let $T$ be any Polish space, $\Borel$ its $\sigma$-algebra of Borel sets.
	Let $(\Omega, \sigAlg, \Prb)$ be a probability space, $Y:\Omega\longrightarrow T$ a $(\sigAlg,\Borel)$-measurable function and $\mathcal{C}$ any sub-$\sigma$-algebra of $\sigAlg$.
	Then there exists a conditional distribution $\Prb_{Y|\mathcal{C}}(\cdot, \cdot \cdot):\Borel \times \Omega \longrightarrow [0,1]$ (see \cite[Chapter 10.2,first definition]{dudley}) and it is unique, meaning that if another $\Prb'(\cdot, \cdot \cdot)$ satisfies the definition of conditional distribution, then
	\begin{equation*}
		\forall^\Prb \omega\in\Omega.
		\quad
		\Prb_{Y|\mathcal{C}}(\cdot, \omega) = \Prb'(\cdot, \omega)
	\end{equation*}
\end{theorem}

In other words, given a sub-$\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{C}$ of $\sigAlg$ and a \rv $Y:(\Omega, \sigAlg, \Prb) \longrightarrow (T,\Borel)$, a sufficient condition for $\PrbCond{Y \in \cdot}{\mathcal{C}}$ to behave like a measure for $\Prb_{|\mathcal{C}}$-almost every $\omega$ is that the codomain $T$ is a Polish space, \ie

\begin{definition}
	Let $(T, \tau)$ be a topological space. $T$ is \emph{Polish} iff $\tau$ is metrizable with respect to a metric $d$ such that $(T,d)$ is a complete separable metric space.
\end{definition}

Then the regularity of our measures is depending not on the $\sigma$-algebra on which we are conditioning, but on the codomain of the process which is being conditioned, which must be Polish.
Most of the processes we take into account are taking values into some subset of \ref{app:def:space-of-solutions} of the form

\begin{equation*}
	\left\{
	f:\R_+ \longrightarrow
	(\R^d)^n\times(\confCamp(d))^k
	:\
	f \text{ is \cadlag}
	\right\}
\end{equation*}

for some $n,k \in \N_0$.
This is an idea of how somebody might try to prove that this space is Polish:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Prove that $\{\spadesuit\} \sqcup \R^d$ is Polish.
	This can be done by viewing $\{\spadesuit\} \sqcup \R^d$ as a metrized one-point-compactification of $\R^d$ (since $\R^d$ is Polish one can use \cite[Theorem 5.3]{kechris} to obtain the metric), which then is separable (compact and metrizable).
	Since then $\{\spadesuit\} \sqcup \R^d$ is metrizable and compact, by \cite[Theorem 45.1]{munkres-2000} we obtain that it is complete, and thus a Polish space.
	\item Prove that $\confCamp(d)$ is Polish. 
	One might want to generalize the result \cite[Theorem 43.4]{munkres-2000} for $\{\spadesuit\} \sqcup \R^d$ to ensure that the countable product $(\{\spadesuit\} \sqcup \R^d)^\N$ is complete.
	In such case, since the metric $\rho$ of the latter theorem induces the product topology, we get that by the Tychonoff theorem (\cite[Theorem 37.3]{munkres-2000}) the space $(\{\spadesuit\} \sqcup \R^d)^\N$ is also compact.
	Being compact and metrizable we obtain that is also separable, and therefore Polish.
	\item Since both the completeness and the separability remain stable under a finite product we obtain that $(\R^d)^n\times(\confCamp(d))^k$ is Polish.
	\item Defining $E:=(\R^d)^n\times(\confCamp(d))^k$ we have that the space
	\begin{equation*}
		\left\{
		f:\R_+ \longrightarrow
		(\R^d)^n\times(\confCamp(d))^k
		:\
		f \text{ is \cadlag}
		\right\}
	\end{equation*}
	can be seen as the \emph{Skorokhod space} $\skorokhod{E}$ (see \cite[Chapter 3, Section 5]{kurtz}), which, if $E$ is Polish, is also Polish (\cite[Chapter 3, Theorem 5.6]{kurtz}).
\end{enumerate}

We expect then that the all conditional probability measures are regular, since we expect that the processes considered have Polish spaces as codomains.
