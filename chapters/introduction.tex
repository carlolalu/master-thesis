This master's thesis' aim is to analyze mathematically the phenomenon of \emph{chemotaxis}, \ie the movement of an organism in response to a chemical stimulus.
This is a mechanism used by a considerable amount of microorganisms (various bacteria and amoebas as well as some eukaryots) to search for nutrients and escape toxins, but also by cells of a multicellular living beings for more complex tasks (\eg leukocytes rely on it to find infections in the body \cite{chemotaxis-leukocytes}).
Naturally, the study of this process has wide applications in medicine, since the variation of the chemotactical behaviour of various human cells can help the onset of a number of illnesses (\cite{ct-diseases}), but also because many drugs act by altering the chemotactic behaviour of specific agents (see for example \cite[Conclusions and perspectives]{cancer-medicine-chemotaxis}).
There is considerable research on this theme on a cellular level (how does the microorganism perceive the chemical stimulus? Which receptors are responsible for it? \cite{ct-chemical-mechanisms}), but we are interested to inquire about the behaviour of a population of microorganisms: what happens when all these corpuscles start to follow a specific chemical stimulus? Can we predict what we will observe about the spatial distribution of the group, for example through the lenses of a microscope?

When examining mathematically the phenomenon on such scale, there are two main point of view:
one can see the population macroscopically, as a continuum, representing it therefore with a density $\adens$, or can see it microscopically, \ie as a finite group of individuals $\{\amba^1, \ldots, \amba^N\}$.
Of course it would be optimal that the two descriptions somehow match, meaning that if we construct some empirical density from $\{\amba^1, \ldots, \amba^N\}$, this is in some way close to $\adens$, meaning that one can mathematically derive the macroscopic system from the microscopic one.
For this procedure, which is often seen as adding insights and validity to a macroscopic description, is common to speak of \emph{propagation of chaos} and of \emph{effective equations} of the microscopic model.
This was the topic of Cañizares García in her PhD thesis \cite{ana}, who derived the classical macroscopic model for chemotaxis, the \emph{Keller-Segel} equation, from a microscopic model for the amoeba \emph{Dictyostelium Discoideum}.
The master's thesis focuses on studying what happens if one would construct a microscopic system more general than the one of \cite{ana}, where the molecules of the chemical stimuli are taken into account as a different population of individuals spawned at random times.

\section{Chemotaxis...}

In Ancient Greek "τάξις" means "order" or "arrangement", but in biology it is used to indicate the movement of an organism in response to an external stimulus.
Depending on the type of such trigger the phenomenon is classified into different subcategories, \eg \emph{phototaxis} or \emph{thermotaxis}.
The τάξις can be \emph{positive}, meaning directed towards the stimulus, or \emph{negative}, away from it.
\emph{Chemotaxis} (from the Ancient Greek "χημεία", meaning "chemistry" - or more precisely "transmutation") is the specific case where the reason of the movement is some chemical substance.
Many lifeforms rely on this mechanism for their survival, the most simple examples being unicellular organisms searching for nutrients (\eg the bacterium \emph{Escherichia coli} follows sources of sugar, \cite{ct-ecoli-glucose}) or fleeing toxins (\eg \emph{Escherichia coli} being repelled by acetate, \cite{negative-chemotaxis}).
More evolved paradigms can be observed in multicellular organisms: white blood cells are able to find infections by following formyl peptides, that is molecular byproducts of many bacteria (\cite{ct-immune-system}).
The phenomenon is even more interesting when the \emph{chemotaxin}, the substance triggering the reaction, seems to be released as a form of signaling.
Many kinds of growth are controlled in this way: the axons of neurons develop following extra-cellular signaling substances (\cite{ct-axons-neurons}), as it is the case for the sprouting of new capillaries (\emph{angiogenesis}).
The latter example is particularly relevant in medicine because many tumors secrete the corresponding chemotaxin VEGF (Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor), stimulating the proliferation of blood vessels in their direction and effectively hacking the body machinery to steal nutrients (\cite{ct-cancer-angiogenesis}).
Other important examples from medicine can be found in \cite{ct-diseases}.

\subsection{...and Dictyostelium Discoideum}

In order to study a phenomena occurring so frequently and in so many forms, it is necessary to find a case free of great complications and which can be observed in a controlled environment: the amoeba \emph{Dictyostelium discoideum} is perfect for such a role.
This organism proliferates till there are available nutrients.
When these finish, the cells first disperse, searching for other food resources, but if after a while these are not found, some individuals start to secrete cAMP (3',5'-cyclic adenosine monophosphate).
Such substance act as a chemical signal, triggering the amoebas who come in contact to it to direct their movement towards its higher concentration and to release other cAMP themselves.
This chemotactical mechanism aggregates the microorganisms into many \emph{slugs}, each formed by several thousands members; these lumps then migrate to the appropriate spot thermotactically to undergo the last metamorphosis: they produce \emph{fruiting bodies} which then release spores and die.
The spores become then new individuals, and the life cycle is closed.
Since an image is worthy more than a thousand words, I advise you to take a look at the short documentary \cite{ze-frank} for an informal explanation on this amoeba which is worth more than $10\cdot 3600 \cdot \text{fpm}$ words.

The \emph{Dictyostelium discoideum} is fairly easy to cultivate in a lab (\cite[Chpater 4]{dd-in-lab}), in some stages of its life is haploid (\ie has a single copy of its DNA) which allows for easy genetic manipulation, and its genome (~34MB) is completely mapped and available to the public (\cite{dd-bioinfo-lj}).
At the same time it is an eukaryote and presents complex behaviours, such as cell differentiation and apoptosis (programmed cell death) in the non-unicellular phases, thermotaxis in its slug phase (\cite{thermotaxis-dd}).
And, more relevant to us, it is regarded as one of the basic example for eukaryotic chemotaxis (\cite{chemotaxis-cancer-and-simpler-models}).

\subsection{The classic macroscopic approach: Keller-Segel}

The macroscopic \emph{classic mathematical model for chemotaxis}, namely the Keller-Segel system of equations, was given in 1970 in \cite{keller-segel} (a more beginner friendly and recent source to read about it is \cite{guide-pdes-chemotaxis}), even though an heuristic microscopic derivation had already been given by Patlak in 1953 in \cite{patlak}.
In the specific case in which the chemotaxin molecules diffuse much faster than the organism reacting to them (as it is the case for the aforementioned amoeba), one can derive from the general case this parabolic-elliptic system of equations (\cite{keller-segel-reduced}):

\begin{equation}\label{intro:keller-segel-ana}
	\left\{
	\begin{aligned}
		\partial_t \adens &= - \chi \nabla (\adens \nabla \cdens) + \bigtriangleup \adens
		\\
		-\bigtriangleup \cdens &= \adens
	\end{aligned}
	\right.
\end{equation}

Here $\adens$ represents the density of the microorganisms, $\cdens$ of the chemotaxin and $\chi$ is a constant called \emph{chemosensitivity}, which measures how much the cells are reacting to the chemical stimuli.
Solving analytically for $\cdens$ the system above is resumed in

\begin{equation}\label{intro:keller-segel-ana-1-equation}
	\partial_t \adens = - \chi \nabla ( (\cutoffSinAttraction \star \adens) \adens) + \bigtriangleup \adens
\end{equation}

where $\cutoffSinAttraction:=\frac{x}{2\pi \abs{x}^2}$.

\subsection{The PhD thesis of Cañizares García}

In \cite{ana} the author derived the set of equations \ref{intro:keller-segel-ana} from a microscopic model previously seen in numerical approximation of solutions of Keller-Segel (\cite{numerics-keller-segel}) as well as in other derivation of it (\cite{keller-segel-dd-derivation-previously}).
The microscopic system describes $N$ amoebas with their positions $\{\amba^1, \ldots, \amba^N\}\in\R^2$ and encodes their attraction via cAMP with a Coulomb-like potential $\potential$.
In equations we have

\begin{equation}
		\forall p \in \{1, \ldots, N\}.
		\qquad 
		\amba_t^p = \amba_0^p - \int_0^t \frac{\chi}{N} \sum_{q\neq p} \cutoffSinAttraction (\amba_s^p - \amba_s^q) ds + \sqrt{2} B_t^p,
		\qquad
		\amba_0^p \sim \adens_0
\end{equation}

where $B^p: \R_+ \longrightarrow \R^2$ are \iid \Bms (Brownian motions), and at time $t=0$ the microorganisms $\amba_0^p$ are \iid according to the given density $\adens_0$.
From this system of SDEs she derived the Keller-Segel equations in their parabolic-elliptic form \ref{intro:keller-segel-ana-1-equation}.
This means that she proved, under the appropriate conditions to ensure the existence of nice enough solutions for \ref{intro:keller-segel-ana}, that the empirical measure

\begin{equation*}
	\empmeas_t^{\amba,(N)}:= \frac{1}{N}\sum_{p=1}^N \delta_{\amba_t^p}
\end{equation*}

converges to $\adens_t$ as a \rv (random variable) with values in $\PrbMeas(\R^2)$ (the space of probability measures in $\R^2$) where $\adens_t$ is the solution to \ref{intro:keller-segel-ana-1-equation} with initial condition $\adens_{t=0} = \adens_0$.
Her technique involved the usage of a mesoscopic model given by the \iid \emph{mean-field particles}

\begin{equation}\label{intro:ana-mesoscopic}
	\forall p \in \{1, \ldots, N\}.
	\qquad
	\mfamba_t^p = \mfamba_0^p - \int_0^t \frac{\chi}{N} (\cutoffSinAttraction \star \adens_s )(\mfamba_s^p) ds + \sqrt{2} B_t^p,
	\qquad
	\mfamba_0^p \sim \adens_0
\end{equation}

coupled (\ie with the same \Bm) with the original ones.
She used the fact that the expression \ref{intro:keller-segel-ana-1-equation} is the \emph{Kolmogorov forward equation} (Fokker-Planck) of \ref{intro:ana-mesoscopic} to obtain the convergence of the aforementioned $\PrbMeas(\R^2)$-valued \rvs by showing that the probability of $(\amba^p)_p$ and $(\mfamba^p)_p$ being far tends to $0$ as $N$ grows to infinity.
More precisely, in \cite[Theorem 2.1]{ana} she could prove a degree of convergence of this probability to $0$ faster than $N^{-\gamma}$ for every $\gamma > 0$.
