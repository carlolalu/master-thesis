\section{Introduction}

In the previous chapter we have defined a microscopic system, \ie a system where we track the movement of each individual.
Consider $\sol_t^N = (\amba_t^{(N)}, \camp_t^{(N)})$, the solution to \ref{ch2:model} with $N$ amoebas, where $\amba_t^{(N)}$ represents the $N$ amoebas and $\camp_t^{(N)}$ the cAMP molecules.
Then such solution can be expressed in terms of its empirical measures:

\begin{equation*}
	\begin{aligned}
		\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N} &:= \frac{1}{N}\sum_{p=1}^N \delta_{\amba_t^p}
		\\
		\empmeas_t^{(\camp),N} &:= "\1_{\hmactive_t \neq 0} \frac{1}{\hmactive_t}\sum_{m=1}^{\hmactive_t} \delta_{\camp_t^m} "
	\end{aligned}
\end{equation*}

A macroscopic description of this model for the amoebas' part is a density $\adens(t,\cdot)$ independent form $N$ such that, in some sense

\begin{equation*}
	\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N} \xrightarrow{N\longrightarrow +\infty} \adens(t,\cdot)
\end{equation*}

This description is macroscopic, because we loose the information about each individual, but we still describe the movement of the individuals as a whole.
In this chapter we craft a candidate for $\adens(t,\cdot)$ and we explain the condition above and how to prove it in a more handy way.
Specifically we build a mesoscopic model, \ie a model between the macroscopic and the microscopic one, which serves as a bridge.
Finally we hint at the idea of finding a similar macroscopic description for the cAMP part, namely a density $\cdens(t,\cdot)$ such that

\begin{equation*}
	\empmeas_t^{(\camp),N} \xrightarrow{N\longrightarrow +\infty} \cdens(t,\cdot)
\end{equation*}


\section{Weak convergence and convergence of empirical measures}

We follow \cite[Section 11.4]{dudley} and \cite[Part 1, Ch. 6]{cedric-villani-opt-trans}.
In general, having a sequence of probability measures $(\mu_N)_N$ and a probability measure $\mu$ on $\R^d$, the sequence is converging (weakly) to $\mu$ iff

\begin{equation*}
	\forall f \in \Cspace_b(\R^d). \qquad \mu_N(f) := \int f d\mu_N \xrightarrow{N} \mu(f) = \int f d\mu
\end{equation*}

\ie we see the space of probability measures $\PrbMeas(\R^d)$ as the dual space of $\Cspace_b(\R^d)$ endowed with the weak-* topology, where $\Cspace_b(\R^d)$ is the space of bounded continuous functions $f:E \longrightarrow \R$ endowed with the norm $\Norm{\cdot}_\infty$.
This definition cannot be directly applied to our case, because the empirical measures are \emph{random}, \ie are \rv mapping into the space of probability measures.
For this to be a valid definition we need to have a $\sigma$-algebra on $\PrbMeas(\R^d)$:

\begin{definition}
	The \emph{weak Borel $\sigma$-algebra on $\PrbMeas(\R^d)$} is the $\sigma$ algebra generated by the weak-* topology on $\PrbMeas(\R^d) = (\Cspace_b(\R^d))^\star$, \ie the coearset $\sigma$-algebra such that
		\begin{equation*}
			\forall f \in \BL(\R^d).\qquad
			\phi_f: \PrbMeas(\R^d) \longrightarrow \R_+, \qquad
			\phi_f(\mu) := \int f d\mu\quad
			\text{ is measurable}
		\end{equation*}
\end{definition}

Then the condition that we actually need to show is the convergence in law of the \rv $\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N}$ to the (constant) \rv $\adens_t$:

\begin{equation}\label{ch3:cumbersome-condition-convergence-in-Pspace}
	\begin{aligned}
	\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N} \xrightarrow{\text{in law in $\PrbMeas(\R^d)$}} \adens_t
	\qquad \Longleftrightarrow
	\quad
	&\forall F\in \Cspace_b(\PrbMeas(\R^d)).\quad
	\E{F(\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N})}
	\xrightarrow{N \rightarrow \infty} 
	\E{F(\adens_t)}
	\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

This condition is cumbersome, and we can do better than it.
Thanks to the theorem \cite[Proposition 2.2]{sznitman} we can find a less scary condition:

\begin{proposition}\label{ch3:proposition:sznitman}
	Let $\amba^{(N)} := (\amba^1, \ldots, \amba^N)$ be exchangeable\footnotemark[1] \rv in $\R^{dN}$, let $\Psi^N\in \PrbMeas(\R^{dN})$ be its law and $\Psi^{N, (k)}$ the $k$-th marginal, \ie
	\begin{equation*}
		\Psi^{N, (k)} (x) := \int_{\R^{d(N-k)}} \Psi^N dx_{k+1} \ldots dx_N
	\end{equation*}
	Let $\empmeas^{(\amba),N}$ its empirical measure.
	Then for a measure $\adens \in \PrbMeas(\R^d)$ are equivalent the following statements:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item The \rv $\empmeas^{(\amba),N}$ with values in $\PrbMeas(\R^d)$ converges in law to the constant \rv $\adens \in \PrbMeas(\R^d)$.
		\item For all $k \geq 1$ the marginal $\Psi^{N, (k)}$ converges weakly to $ \otimes_{p=1}^N \adens$ in $N$.
		\item The $2$-marginal $\Psi^{N, (2)}$ converges weakly to $ \adens\otimes\adens$ in $N$.
	\end{itemize}
\end{proposition}
\footnotetext[1]{Meaning that under each permutation $\pi$ the $(\amba^{\pi(1)}, \ldots, \amba^{\pi(N)})$ has the same law as $\amba^(N) := (\amba^1, \ldots, \amba^N)$, a condition fullfilled by the system \ref{ch2:model} for every $t$.}

In this way we must "only" show the weak convergence of the $k$-marginals $\Psi^{N, (k)}$ to $ \otimes_{p=1}^N \adens$.
Then we will need another instrument: a suitable metric for the topology of the weak convergence of measures (that, as we have seen, is the weak-* topology on $\Cspace_b(\R^d)$). If we restrict the space of allowed measures we can choose the \emph{$p$-Wasserstein distance}, also known as \emph{$p$-Kantorovich–Rubinstein}. Another popular name is \emph{Earth-mover distance} (the latter one can be generalized to finite measures) because of the heuristic that we will see in \ref{ch3:fig:example-coupling}.
To see its definition we first we need the concept of \emph{coupling} of measures.

\begin{definition}
	Consider the space of Borel probability measures on $\R^u$
	\begin{equation}
		\BorelPrbMeas := \BorelPrbMeas (\R^u) := 
		\{\mu :
		\mu \text{ is a \emph{Borel} prob. meas. on } \R^u
		\}
	\end{equation}
	and let $\mu, \nu \in \PrbMeas$. A \emph{coupling of $\mu$ and $\nu$} is a measure $\gamma \in \PrbMeas (\R^u \times \R^u)$ such that for all Borel-measurable and bounded functions $\phi$ we have
	\begin{equation}
		\int_{\R^u \times \R^u} \phi(x) \,d\gamma(x,y) = \int_{\R^u} \phi(x) \,d\mu(x) 
		\quad \text{ and } \quad
		\int_{\R^u \times \R^u} \phi(y) \,d\gamma(x,y) = \int_{\R^u} \phi(y) \,d\nu(x) 
	\end{equation}\label{ch3:def-coupling}
	We sign as $\Gamma(\mu,\nu)$ the set of couplings of $\mu$ and $\nu$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
	The set $\Gamma(\mu,\nu)$ is never empty because the product measure $\mu \otimes \nu$ is always in it.
\end{remark}

A way to visualise a coupling is as a transformation of $\mu$ into $\nu$.
Assume, for the sake of simplicity, that $\mu,\nu$ are measures on $\R$ and are absolute continuous with respect to the Lebesgue measure $\lebsge$.
Consider the densities $\frac{d\mu}{d\lebsge}(x), \frac{d\nu}{d\lebsge}(x)$ and think of their graphs as piles of earth (see \ref{ch3:fig:example-coupling}).

\begin{figure}[h]
	\begin{tikzpicture}
	\end{tikzpicture}
	\caption{[[HERE DRAW A TIKZ THE EARTH MOVING STUFF INTO 2 DIMENSION]]}
\end{figure}\label{ch3:fig:example-coupling}

A plan to move earth in $\R$ can be thought as a function $\overline{\gamma}: \R \times \R \longrightarrow \R $ which with the value $\overline{\gamma}(x,y)=: \overline{\gamma}(x\rightarrow y)$ tells how much earth we should move from the point $x$ to the point $y$.
In order for this to be a plan which precisely transforms $\frac{d\mu}{d\lebsge}$ into $\frac{d\nu}{d\lebsge}$, it must respect two symmetrical conditions:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item For each $x\in\R$ the total earth (mass of) moved from it by the plan must be exactly equal to the earth present in $x$ at the beginning, namely:
	\begin{equation}
		\int \overline{\gamma}(x,y) \,dy = \frac{d\mu}{d\lebsge}(x)
	\end{equation}
	\item For each $y\in\R$ the earth (the mass) moved to it must be exactly equal to the earth present in $y$ at the end, namely:
	\begin{equation}
		\int \overline{\gamma}(x,y) \,dx = \frac{d\nu}{d\lebsge}(y)
	\end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

This is equivalent to \ref{ch3:def-coupling}, with $\overline{\gamma}$ playing the role of the density of the coupling $\gamma$.
With this example in mind we could ask ourselves which is the best among such transformations of $\mu$ into $\nu$.
To do that we need to associate to each coupling $\gamma \in \Gamma(\mu,nu)$ a value, which indicates how "good" a plan $\gamma$ might be.
If we have a notion of distance $dist$ on our space $\R^u$, then a plan can be evaluated with how much net distance we cover to transport all the earth around, \ie with the expression

\begin{equation}
	\int dist(x,y) \,d\gamma(x,y)
\end{equation}

In this way we can say that two measures $\mu,\nu$ are near to each others if we can find a coupling $\gamma \in \Gamma(\mu,\nu)$ such that its evaluation is very low.
With this idea in mind, choosing the distance induced by the normalised $1$-norm on $\R^u$ (meaning $\frac{\norm{\cdot}_1}{u}$), one reaches the definition of the $1$-Wassertein distance (with respect to the normalised $1$-norm).

\begin{definition}
	Consider the space of Borel probability measures with finite $1$-moments on $\R^u$
	\begin{equation}
		\BorelPrbMeas_1 := \BorelPrbMeas_1(\R^u) := 
		\{\mu \in \BorelPrbMeas :
		\int \norm{x} d \mu (x) < \infty
		\} \subseteq \BorelPrbMeas
	\end{equation}
	Then the $1$-Wasserstein distance between two measures $\mu,\nu \in \BorelPrbMeas_1$ is defined as
	\begin{equation}
		W_1(\mu,\nu):= \inf_{\gamma \in \Gamma(\mu,\nu)}
		\int_{\R^u \times \R^u} \frac{1}{u}\sum_{i=1}^{u} \norm{x_i-y_i} d\gamma(x,y)
	\end{equation}
\end{definition}

To see the proof that $W_1$ is a distance take a look at the proof after \autocite[Example 6.3]{cedric-villani-opt-trans}.
The reason why we cover this concept is this result (from \autocite[Corollary 6.13]{cedric-villani-opt-trans}):

\begin{theorem}
	If $(X,d)$ is a Polish space, and $\overline{d}$ is a bounded distance inducing the same topology on $X$ as $d$, then the convergence of measures in the $1$-Wasserstein sense for $\overline{d}$ is equivalent to the usual weak convergence of probability measures in $\BorelPrbMeas_1(\R^u)$.
\end{theorem}

Then we have the subsequent statement:

\begin{lemma}\label{ch3:lemma-sznitman-wasserstein}
	Let $\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N},\adens_t, \Psi_t^{N, (k)},\Psi_t^{N}$ be as in \ref{ch3:proposition:sznitman} and let $\forall k\geq 1. \Psi_t^{N, (k)},\Psi_t^{N} \in \BorelPrbMeas_1$.
	Then the condition
	\begin{equation}\label{ch3:w-1-convergence-condition}
		\sup_{0\leq t\leq T}
		W_1 \big(
		\Psi_t^{N, (k)}, \bigotimes_{p=1}^{k}\adens_t
		\big),
		\quad
		\sup_{0\leq t\leq T}
		W_1 \big(
		\Psi_t^{N}, \bigotimes_{p=1}^{N}\adens_t
		\big),
		\xrightarrow {N \rightarrow \infty} 0
	\end{equation}
	implies that the \rv $\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N}$ with values in $\PrbMeas(\R^d)$ converges in law to the constant \rv $\adens_t \in \PrbMeas(\R^d)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{remark}
	Notice that the condition that $\forall k\geq 1. \Psi_t^{N, (k)},\Psi_t^{N} \in \BorelPrbMeas_1$ is not particularly strict, it translates to requiring that the amoebas do not have the possibility to have an infinite velocity.
\end{remark}

\begin{proof}
	By \ref{ch3:proposition:sznitman} we have that the condition
	\begin{equation*}
		\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N} \xrightarrow{\text{in law in $\PrbMeas(\R^d)$}} \adens_t
	\end{equation*}
	is equivalent to ask that for all $k \geq 1$, the marginals $\Psi^{N, (k)}$ converge weakly to $ \otimes_{p=1}^N \adens$ in $N$.
	Since for every $k\geq 1$ we have that $\Psi_t^{N, (k)} \in \BorelPrbMeas_1$, and since $\BorelPrbMeas_1$ is a Polish space, then also $\lim_N \Psi_t^{N, (k)} = \bigotimes_{p=1}^{k} \adens_t$  is an element of $\BorelPrbMeas_1$.
	Since the $1$-Wasserstein distance $W_1$ is a metric for the weak-convergence of measures, this implies that for every $k\geq 1$ we have that $\Psi_t^{N, (k)}$ converges weakly to $\bigotimes_{p=1}^{k} \adens_t$.
	By the proposition \ref{ch3:proposition:sznitman}, this implies that the \rv $\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N}$ converges in law to $\adens_t$.
\end{proof}

\section{From effective equations to mean-field particles}

We want to find a good candidate $\adens_t := a(t, \cdot)$, but we have no analytical description of the solution of \ref{ch2:model}, we have only its evolution in time.
Effective equations are description of the evolution of the macroscopic description, and this is what realistically we are searching for, \ie a partial differential equation

\begin{equation*}
	\partial_t \adens_t = (\text{some function}) (t, \adens_t)
\end{equation*}

satisfied by $\adens_t$, the macroscopic description of our system.
More precisely, we expect this to happen up to the imposition of the same initial conditions, namely when we also impose that $\adens(0, \cdot) = \adens_0$, where $\adens_0$ is the density of $\amba_0^p$ imposed in the model \ref{ch2:model}.
This might work, but we do not know yet how to find a candidate, and even if we had one, it would still be cumbersome to work with it:
since we have no analytical solution for the model \ref{ch2:model}, we cannot work directly with the marginals $\Psi_t^N$, and we are thus stuck with the empirical measure

\begin{equation*}
\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N} := \frac{1}{N}\sum_{p=1}^N \delta_{\amba_t^p}
\end{equation*}

which is a discrete object.
On the other side we have $\adens_t$, which is a continuous guy.
Then we need a bridge from the PDE-world to the SDE-world: the Fokker-Planck equation (refer for example to \autocite[Theorem 8.1.1]{oksendal}).
Given an SDE

\begin{equation}\label{ch3:SDE-for-Fokker-Planck}
	\mfamba_t = \adens_0 + \int_0^t \mfdrift(t, \mfamba_t) \,dt + \int_0^t \mfdiffusion(t, X_t) \,d B_t
\end{equation}

if this has a solution $\mfamba_t$, then its density $\adens(t, \cdot)$ satifies the PDE

\begin{equation*}\label{ch3:fokker-planck-PDE}
	\left\{
		\begin{aligned}
			\partial_t \adens(t,x) 
			&=  - \sum_{i=1}^{d} \partial_{x_i}[ \mfdrift^i(t,x) \adens(t,x)] 
			+ \sum_{i=1}^{d} \sum_{j=1}^{d} \frac{\partial^2}{\partial_{x_i}\partial_{x_j}} [D_{ij}(t,x) \adens(t,x)]
			\\
			\adens(0,x) &= \adens_0
			\\
			D_{ij}
			&:= \frac{1}{2} \sum_{k} \mfdiffusion^{ik}\mfdiffusion^{jk} = \frac{1}{2} (\mfdiffusion \mfdiffusion^T)_{ij}
		\end{aligned}
	\right.
\end{equation*}

The viceversa is also true: if we encounter a PDE of the form \ref{ch3:fokker-planck-PDE} with solution $\adens_t$, then we can observe the system \ref{ch3:SDE-for-Fokker-Planck} knowing that its solution has $\adens_t$ as density. 
The idea is, in simple words, to exploit this to build somehow a system of \iid $N$ particles 

\begin{equation}
	\mfamba_t^{(N)}=(\mfamba_t^p)_{p=1}^N
\end{equation}

such that each $\mfamba_t^p$ solves a system of the form \ref{ch3:SDE-for-Fokker-Planck} and which for every $t$ are with a high probability particularly close (in space) to the solutions $\amba_t^{(N)}=(\amba_t^p)_{p=1}^N$ of the system \ref{ch2:model}.
In this way, if $\mfamba_t^{(N)}=(\mfamba_t^p)_{p=1}^N$ are \iid, the marginals of $\mfamba_t^{(N)}$ are exactly $\bigotimes_{p=1}^{N}\adens_t$ and we then just need to show that this closeness in space for every time $t$ implies a closeness of the marginals of the two groups of particles, fullfilling \ref{ch3:lemma-sznitman-wasserstein} (all this is precisely stated and proven at \ref{ch3:best-condition-convergence}).
Since this new system $\mfamba_t^{(N)}$ would be between the microscopic description and the macroscopic one, it is often referred to as \emph{mesoscopic description/model}.
More intuitively, if we have such particles $\mfamba_t^{(N)}$ remaining close enough to the original ones $\amba_t^{(N)}$, we should expect their respective empirical densities 

\begin{equation*}
	\empmeas_t^{(\mfamba),N} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{p=1}^N \delta_{\mfamba_t^p},\qquad 
	\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N} := \frac{1}{N}\sum_{p=1}^N \delta_{\amba_t^p}
\end{equation*}

to be close, and thus tend to the same limit in $N$, which we would expect to be the same one given by the Fokker-Planck equation.
The same concept will be roughly applied also to the cAMP, meaning that, under the conditional probability measure $\PrbCondTimes{\cdot}{}$, we will search also for $\hmactive_t$ independent particles $\mfcamp_t^{(N)}$ remaining close to the original $\camp_t^{(N)}$ (and obtaining then also a PDE for the cAMP part).
This not only because it is conceptually more useful to  think in two entire systems of both amoebas and cAMP being close to each others, but also because we hope that it is possible to show, in further work, that this implies also the convergence of measures for the cAMP part, \ie that there is a density $\cdens_t$ easily derivable (with Fokker-Planck and other tools) from $\mfcamp_t^{(N)}$ such that 

\begin{equation*}
	\empmeas_t^{(\camp),N}, \empmeas_t^{(\mfcamp),N} \xrightarrow{N\longrightarrow +\infty} \cdens(t,\cdot)
\end{equation*}

where $\empmeas_t^{(\mfcamp),N}$ is the empirical measure of $\mfcamp_t^{(N)}$.
Then, practically, we are trying to craft a system of $N+\hmactive_t$ particles $\mfsol_t^N = (\mfamba_t^{(N)}, \mfcamp_t^{(N)})$ with these characteristics:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item The particles $\mfsol_t^N = (\mfamba_t^{(N)}, \mfcamp_t^{(N)})$ should be coupled with their counterparts $\sol_t^N = (\amba_t^{(N)}, \camp_t^{(N)})$ (\ie they should have the same \Bm in their stochastic evolution), in order to control more easily the distance between the two.
	Indeed in such case the distance process $\sol_t^N -\mfsol_t^N$ is a finite variation process and can be thus more easily treated, depending only on the two drifts, and not on the diffusions.
	\item The single particles $\mfamba_t^1, \ldots, \mfamba_t^N$ must be \iid so that on each one of them can be described by the same SDE and thus we can use Fokker-Planck separately to have $N$ identical densities $\adens_t^p = \adens_t$, and have thus that the marginals of this system are $\bigotimes_{p=1}^{k}\adens_t$.	
	\item The distance process $\sol_t^N -\mfsol_t^N$ should be well-defined, and thus under the conditional probability $\PrbCondTimes{\cdot}{}$ the processes $\sol_t^N$ and $\mfsol_t^N$ must have the same dimension, meaning each having at every time the same number of cAMP molecules spawned.	
	\item It would be nice that under the conditional probability $\PrbCondTimes{\cdot}{}$ the particles $\mfcamp_t^1, \ldots, \mfcamp_t^{\hmactive_t}$ would be at least independent.
	It would be desirable to have them even \iid to easily apply Fokker-Planck also in this case, but this is not possible.
	In fact, if we want them to be coupled with their counterparts $\camp_t^{p,g}$, we must necessarily have that they move of quasi-\Bm spawning at different random times, which automatically makes them differently distributed.
	As we will see in the paragraph \ref{ch3:spawing-position-parameters}, we will not be able even to obtain the independence.
\end{enumerate}

Till now we found conditions on the mesoscopic system, but we still have not pointed out at the main way to provide our smart guess: the idea is that the macroscopic behaviour is dictated by the \emph{average} dynamic of the system, more precisely, by the \emph{mean} field felt by each amoeba $\amba_t^p$ and cAMP molecule $\camp_t^{p,g}$):

\begin{align*}
	\mfdrift_p^N(t, x) &:= \ECondTimes{\drift_p^N(t, \mfsol_t) }{\mfsol_t^{N,p} = x}
	\\
	\mfdrift_{p,g}^N(t, x) &:= \ECondTimes{\drift_{p,g}^N(t, \mfsol_t) }{\mfsol_t^{N,p,g} = x}
\end{align*}

This in fact guarantees that the force felt by a particle $p$ is dependent only on itself, and thus the independence. Besides then the SDE becomes the same for each amoeba, guaranteeing identical distribution for the amoebas' part.
Then, imposing the same initial condition as for \ref{ch2:model}, the candidate for our system would be something of the form

\begin{equation}\label{eq:approximate-mean-field-equations}
	\left\{
	\begin{aligned}
		\mfamba^p_t &= \adens_0 + \int_0^t \mfdrift^N_p(s,\mfamba_s^k) d s + \sigma_\amba B_t^{\amba,k} &
		\\
		\mfcamp^{p,g}_t &= \text{ (some spawning position } P_{\btime^{p,g}}^{p,g})) + \int_0^t \mfdrift^N_{p,g}(s,\mfcamp_s^{p,g}) d s + \sigma_\camp Q_t^{p,g}\qquad &\text{from time }\btime^{p,g}
	\end{aligned}
	\right.
\end{equation}

We call such particles the \emph{mean-field particles}, for obvious reasons.

\subsection{Calculate the mean-field and guess the spawning position}

First we notice that since the drift of the cAMP molecules is $\equiv 0$, their average drift field is also 0, \ie

\begin{align*}
\forall p,g. \quad \mfdrift_{p,g}^{N}(t, \mfsol_t^{p,g}) 
:= \ECond {\drift_{p,g}^{N}(t, \mfsol_t) }{\mfsol_t^{p,g}}\
&=\ECond {\ \ECondTimes{ \drift_{p,g}^{N}(t, \mfsol_t) }{\mfsol_t^{p,g}, }\ }{\mfsol_t^{p,g}}
\\
&= \E {\ECondTimes{ 0 }{} }  = 0
\end{align*}

Then, with respect to the measure $\PrbCondTimes{\cdot}{}$, the Fokker-Planck equation (\ref{ch3:fokker-planck-PDE}) for such particle will be

\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
\cdens^{\btime^{p,g}}(0, x) 
&= 0 
&&
\\
\partial_t \cdens^{\btime^{p,g}}(t, x) 
&= 0 
&&\text{if } t < \btime^{p,g}
\\
\cdens^{\btime^{p,g}}(\btime^{p,g}, x) 
&= \text{ some spawning position related to } \adens(\btime^{p,g}, \cdot)
&&
\\
\partial_t \cdens^{\btime^{p,g}}(t, x) 
&= D_\camp \bigtriangleup \cdens^{\btime^{p,g}}(t, x) 
&&\text{if } t \geq \btime^{p,g}
\\
D_\camp
&:= \frac{1}{2} \sum_{k=1}^{d} \sigma_\camp^2 = \frac{d\sigma_\camp^2}{2}
&&
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}

We could expect to describe the initial density at the spawning $\cdens^{\btime^{p,g}}(\btime^{p,g}, \cdot)$ as simply $\adens(\btime^{p,g}, \cdot)$, since the latter one should represent the spatial distribution of the amoebas.
Then taking considering the arbitrary birth time as a parameter $\tau = \btime^{p,g}$ we can write

\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
\cdens^\tau(t, x) 
& \equiv 0
&&\text{if } t < \tau
\\
\cdens^\tau(\tau, \cdot) 
&= \adens(\tau, \cdot)
&&
\\
\partial_t \cdens^\tau
&= D_\camp \bigtriangleup \cdens^\tau
&&\text{if } t \geq \tau
\\
D_\camp
&:= \frac{1}{2} \sum_{k=1}^{d} \sigma_\camp^2 = \frac{d\sigma_\camp^2}{2}
&&
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}\label{ch3:eq:eff-eq-candidate-camp}

This is simply a heat-equation starting with initial condition $\adens(\tau)$ at time $\tau$. 
Therefore, considering the $d$-dimensional heat-kernel $\heatkernel_t^{D_\camp}$ (modified so that it is $\equiv 0$ for negative values of $t$) we can express it with the analytical solution

\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
\cdens^\tau(\tau, x) 
&= \adens(\tau)
\\
\cdens^\tau(t, x) 
&= 
	\left(
		\heatkernel_{t-\tau}^{D_\camp} \star \adens(\tau, \cdot) 
	\right) (x)
\\
\heatkernel_s^{D_\camp}(x) &:= \frac{1}{
		\big( 4 D_\camp \pi s \big)^{\frac{d}{2}}
	}
	exp \left(

where $\star$ is the convolution in space.

\paragraph{The cAMP spawning positions} \label{ch3:spawing-position-parameters}
We require that the spawning position $P_{\btime^{p,g}}^{p,g}$ of $\mfcamp_t^{p,g}$ is $\adens_{\btime^{p,g}}$ distributed.
Then $P_t^{p,g}$ fullfills the same SDE \ref{ch3:eff-equ-amoebas} as the mean-field amoebas do.
Furthermore we need that the distance process 
\begin{equation*}
	\camp_t^{p,g} - \mfcamp_t^{p,g}
	=
	\amba_{\btime^{p,g}}^p + \diffusion_\camp \quasibm_t^{p,g} - (P_{\btime^{p,g}}^p + \diffusion_\camp \quasibm_t^{p,g}) 
	= 
	\amba_{\btime^{p,g}}^p - P_{\btime^{p,g}}^p
\end{equation*}
is a finite variation process to easily treat it in the section \ref{ch4:lemma:get-rid-of-integral}. 
Then $P_t^p$ must be coupled with $\amba_t^p$, and therefore it must necessarily be that $P_t^p =  \mfamba_t^p$, \ie the cAMP particles fullfill the SDE
\begin{equation*}
	\mfcamp_t^{p,g} = \mfamba_{\btime^{p,g}}^p + \sigma_\camp \quasibm_t^{p,g} \qquad\text{ from time }\btime^{p,g}
\end{equation*}
In other words we are forced to give up the independence for the particles $(\mfcamp_t^{p,g})_{g}$ (with a fixed $p$), but we still have that (for a fixed $g$) the particles $(\mfcamp_t^{p,g})_{p}$ are \iid.
Then our whole system of mean-field particles is given by

\begin{equation}\label{ch3:MF-particles}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
\mfamba_t^p &= \adens_0 + \int_0^t \left( 
		\attraction \star \int_0^s \lambda \cdens_s^\tau 	d \tau
	\right) (\mfamba_s^p) ds + \diffusion_\amba B_t^p
\\
\mfcamp_t^{p,g} &= \mfamba_{\btime^{p,g}}^p + \sigma_\camp \quasibm_t^{p,g} &&\text{ from time }\btime^{p,g}
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}

where $\cdens_t^\tau$ is defined by the correspondent effective equations:

\begin{equation}\label{ch3:EFFECTIVE-EQS}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
\adens(0, \cdot) &= \adens_0
\\
\partial_t \adens &= \nabla \left[ \left( \nabla \potential
				\star \int_0^t \lambda \cdens_t^\tau 	d \tau
			\right)
			\adens
			\right]
	+ \frac{d\sigma_\amba^2}{2} \bigtriangleup \adens	
\\
\cdens^\tau(t, \cdot) 
& \equiv 0
&&\text{if } t < \tau
\\
\cdens^\tau(\tau, \cdot) 
&= \adens(\tau, \cdot)
&&
\\
\partial_t \cdens^\tau
&= \frac{d\sigma_\camp^2}{2} \bigtriangleup \cdens^\tau
&&\text{if } t \geq \tau
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Solution of the derived systems and their properties}

The system \ref{ch3:integro-differential-system} is a non-linear partial integro-differential equation (non-linear PIDE).
In other words, it is a pretty wild beast.
The best instrument to prove that it does possess a solution is the Schauder fixed point theorem (see \autocite[Exercise 6.26]{brezis}, which does not guarantee unicity:

\begin{theorem}\label{ch3:fixed-point-schauder-theorem}
Let $E$ be a Banach space and let $C \subseteq E$ be a non-empty closed convex subset of $E$.
Let $F: C \longrightarrow C$ be a $\Cspace^0$ map such that $F(C) \subseteq K$, where $K\subseteq C$ is a compact subset of $C$.
Then $F$ has a fixed point in $K$.
\end{theorem}

The usual procedure to apply the theorem is to separate the linear part from the non-linear one, to invert the linear operator with the green function of the linear operator, and then to verify that, with a domain in the space of functions with the characteristics that we do desire, the operator respects the hypothesis of the theorem \ref{ch3:fixed-point-schauder-theorem}.
This is not proved in this master thesis, but it is assumed that the solution exist.
Notice furthermore that the existence of a sufficiently regular solution $\adens_t$ to the system \ref{ch3:integro-differential-system} is equivalent to require the existence of solutions $\adens_t$ and $\cdens_t^\tau$ for each $\tau \in \R_+$ to \ref{ch3:EFFECTIVE-EQS}.

With this view on the problem we make this assumptions regarding the solutions $\adens_t, \cdens_t$ of the system \ref{ch3:EFFECTIVE-EQS}:
\begin{enumerate}\label{ch3:conditions-on-eff-equ-solutions}
	\item There are solution $\adens_t,\cdens_t^\tau \in \Lpspace^1(\R^d)$ (for every $\tau \geq0$) for \ref{ch3:integro-differential-system} and it is valid that $\int \adens_t dx = 1= \int \cdens_t^\tau dx$, meaning that $\forall t$ they are probability distributions.
	\item The solutions $\adens_t,\cdens_t^\tau$ are not exploding to infinity in finite time, meaning that there is an increasing, positive and continuous function $\densBound(T)=:\densBound_T$ in $T$ such that 
	\begin{equation*}
		\sup_{t\leq T} \Norm{\adens_t}_{\Lpspace^\infty(\R^d)}, \sup_{t\leq T} \Norm{\cdens_t}_{\Lpspace^\infty(\R^d)} \leq \densBound_T 
	\end{equation*}
	This condition will be necessary to guarantee the existence of solutions for the mean-field particle system \ref{ch3:MF-particles} and to prove the law of large numbers at \ref{ch5:LLN}.
\end{enumerate}

Now that the mean-field particles are well-defined we can finally treat the condition we are gonna use to prove the convergence of the empirical measures of the amoebas of the original system \ref{ch2:model} towards the distribution $\adens_t$.

		&=
		\E{
			\norm{
				\adens_0 + \int_0^t \drift(s, \mfamba_s^p) ds + \diffusion_\amba B_t^p
				-
				\left(
				\adens_0
				+ \int_0^t \mfdrift(s, \mfamba_s^p) d s
				+ 
				\diffusion_\amba B_t^p
				\right)
			}_\infty
			\1_{\Xi_t}
		} + 
		N^{-\cutparameter}
		\\
		&=
		\E{
			\norm{
				\int_0^t
					\drift(s, \mfamba_s^p) - \mfdrift(s, \mfamba_s^p)
				d s
			}_\infty
			\1_{\Xi_t}
		}
		+ 
		N^{-\cutparameter}\
		\leq\
		\E{
			\int_0^t 
			\Norm{
				\drift(s, \cdot)
			}_\infty
			+
			\Norm{
			 	\mfdrift(s, \cdot)
			}_\infty
			ds\
			\1_{\Xi_t}
		}
		+
		N^{-\cutparameter}
	\end{align*}
	
	Recalling now the bound for the drift (\ref{ch2:lemma:lipsch-of-drift}) and the mean-field drift (\ref{ch3:mfdrift-lips-and-bounded}) we can proceed as following
	
	\begin{align*}
		&\leq
			\E{
				\int_0^t 
				\const_{\attraction}\
				\frac{\hmactive_s}{N}
				+
				\const_{\attraction, \lambda} s\			
				ds\
				\1_{\Xi_t}
			}
			+
			N^{-\cutparameter}\
		\\
		&\leq\
			\E{
				t
				\const_{\attraction}\
				\frac{\hmactive_t}{N}\
				\1_{\Xi_t}
			}
			+
			\E{
				\const_{\attraction, \lambda} 
				\frac{t^2}{2}\
				\1_{\Xi_t}
			}
			+
			N^{-\cutparameter}
		\\
		&=
		\frac{t\const_{\attraction}}{N}\
		\E{
			\hmactive_t
			\1_{\Xi_t}
		}
		+
		\const_{\attraction, \lambda} 
		\frac{t^2}{2}\
		\E{
			\1_{\Xi_t}
		}
		+
		N^{-\cutparameter}
		\leq
	\end{align*}
	
	Now we can use Hölder on $ \E{
		\hmactive_t
		\1_{\Xi_t}
	}$ for some $p,q\in (1, +\infty)$ such that $\frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{q} = 1$ together with the bound on the measure of $\Xi_t$ to obtain that
	
	\begin{align*}
		&\leq\
			\frac{t\const_{\attraction}}{N}\
			(\E{(\hmactive_t)^q})^\frac{1}{q}
				\E{
					\1_{\Xi_t}^p
				}^\frac{1}{p}
			+
			\const_{\attraction, \lambda} 
			t^2\
			\E{
				\1_{\Xi_t}
			}
			+
			N^{-\cutparameter}
			\\
		&\leq\
			\frac{t\const_{\attraction}}{N}\
			(\E{(\hmactive_t)^q})^\frac{1}{q}\
			K^\frac{1}{p}	N^\frac{-(\xi-\cutparameter)}{p}\
			+
			\const_{\attraction, \lambda} 
			t^2\
			K\
			N^{-(\xi-\cutparameter)}
			+
			N^{-\cutparameter}
		\\
		&=\
			\frac{t\
				\const_{\attraction,p}
			}{N}\
			(\E{(\hmactive_t)^q})^\frac{1}{q}\
			N^\frac{-(\xi-\cutparameter)}{p}\
			+
			\const_{\attraction, \lambda} 
			t^2\
			N^{-(\xi-\cutparameter)}
			+
			N^{-\cutparameter}
	\end{align*}
	
	Then, applying the estimate of Poisson processes moments from \ref{app:Poisson-moments} we obtain that
	
	\begin{align*}
		&\leq
			\frac{t\
				\const_{\attraction,p}
			}{N}\
				(\Fup(q)^\frac{1}{q}\: (\lambda Nt)^{q\frac{1}{q}} \vee 1)\
			N^\frac{-(\xi-\cutparameter)}{p}\
			+
			\const_{\attraction, \lambda} 
			t^2\
			N^{-(\xi-\cutparameter)}
			+
			N^{-\cutparameter}
	\end{align*}
	
	Supposing that $\lambda Nt \geq 1$ (which is true for $N$ sufficiently big if $t>0$) we obtain that
	
	\begin{align*}
		&=
				\frac{t\
					\const_{\attraction,p,q, \lambda}
				}{N}\
				(Nt)
				N^\frac{-(\xi-\cutparameter)}{p}\
			+
				\const_{\attraction, \lambda} 
				t^2\
				N^{-(\xi-\cutparameter)}
			+
				N^{-\cutparameter}
		\\
			&\leq
				\const_{\attraction,p,q, \lambda}\
				t^2\
				N^\frac{-(\xi-\cutparameter)}{p}\
				+
				\const_{\attraction, \lambda} 
				t^2\
				N^{-(\xi-\cutparameter)}
				+
				N^{-\cutparameter}
	\end{align*}
	
	Resuming, then, we have that
	
	\begin{equation*}
		W_1 \big(
		\Psi_t^{N}, \bigotimes_{p=1}^{N}\adens_t
		\big) 
		\leq
		\const_{\attraction, \lambda}
		t^2
		N^{-(\xi-\cutparameter)}
		+
		t^2\
		\const_{\attraction,\lambda,q,p}\
		N^\frac{-(\xi-\cutparameter)}{p}
		+
		N^{-\cutparameter}
	\end{equation*}
	
	and thus 
	
	\begin{equation*}
		\sup_{t\in[0,T]} W_1 \big(
		\Psi_t^{N}, \bigotimes_{p=1}^{N}\adens_t
		\big) 
		\leq
		\const_{\attraction, \lambda}
		T^2
		N^{-(\xi-\cutparameter)}
		+
		T^2\
		\const_{\attraction,\lambda,q,p}\
		N^\frac{-(\xi-\cutparameter)}{p}
		+
		N^{-\cutparameter}
		\xrightarrow{N\rightarrow +\infty} 0
	\end{equation*}
	
	where the limit goes to $0$ because $0< \cutparameter < \xi < \frac{1}{2}$.
\end{proof}


\section{A macroscopic description for the cAMP molecules?}

The cAMP molecules are created at a rate $\lambda$, and each molecule created at a random time $\tau$ has a density fullfilling

\begin{equation*}
	\left\{
\begin{aligned}
	\cdens^\tau(t, \cdot) 
	& \equiv 0
	&&\text{if } t < \tau
	\\
	\cdens^\tau(\tau, \cdot) 
	&= \adens(\tau, \cdot)
	&&
	\\
	\partial_t \cdens^\tau
	&= \frac{d\sigma_\camp^2}{2} \bigtriangleup \cdens^\tau
	&&\text{if } t \geq \tau
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation*}

This, together with the form of the effective equations \ref{ch3:EFFECTIVE-EQS}, where the $\adens_t$ should follow the cAMP by the convolution, seems to suggest that

\begin{equation*}
	"\1_{\hmactive_t \neq 0} \frac{1}{\hmactive_t}\sum_{m=1}^{\hmactive_t} \delta_{\camp_t^m} " =: \empmeas_t^{(\camp),N} 
	\xrightarrow{N\longrightarrow +\infty} 
		\cdens(t,\cdot) :=
		 \int_0^t \lambda \cdens_t^\tau 	d \tau
\end{equation*}

The problem is that the cAMP molecules, both in the microscopic and in the mesoscopic model, are \emph{not} exchangeable, and thus the proposition of Sznitman \ref{ch3:proposition:sznitman} cannot be used, and one might have to manually use the condition \ref{ch3:cumbersome-condition-convergence-in-Pspace}.
There are some properties of the cAMP particles which show somehow symmetry, namely, under the conditional probability $\PrbCondTimes{\cdot}{}$:
\begin{itemize}
	\item The spawning position of each $\camp_t^{p,g}$ is given by $\amba_t^p$, and the amoebas are exchangeable.
	Even better, $\mfcamp_t^{p,g}$ are spawning on $\mfamba_t^p$, and the latter ones are \iid.
	\item Each $\camp_t^{p,g}$ moves of a quasi-\Bm starting at $\btime^{p,g}$, and these birth times are \iid with respect to the parameter $p$. Since the mesoscopic molecules $\mfcamp_t^{p,g}$ are coupled with the microscopic ones, the same happens for them 
\end{itemize}
Because of these facts, for each generation $g$, we have that $(\camp^{p,g})_p$ are exchangeable and $(\mfcamp^{p,g})_p$ are \iid. 
Other nuances come from the fact that the the number of molecules spawned is stochastically distributed with the \rvs $(\hmactive_t^p)_p$, and thus also for the $W_1$-convergence a bit work would be necessary (maybe even approximating first with the conditional probability $\PrbCondTimes{\cdot}{}$ and then with $\Prb$).
Because of this apparent difficulties, and since the key question of this work was focused on the macroscopic description of the amoebas, not a lot of effort has been spent on this topic, leaving it for eventual future works.
