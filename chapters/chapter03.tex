\section{Introduction}

In the previous chapter we have defined a microscopic system, \ie a system where we track the movement of each individual.
Consider $\sol_t^N = (\amba_t^{(N)}, \camp_t^{(N)})$, the solution to \ref{ch2:model} with $N$ amoebas, where $\amba_t^{(N)}$ represents the $N$ amoebas and $\camp_t^{(N)}$ the cAMP molecules.
Then such solution can be expressed in terms of its empirical measures:

\begin{equation*}
	\begin{aligned}
		\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N} &:= \frac{1}{N}\sum_{p=1}^N \delta_{\amba_t^p}
		\\
		\empmeas_t^{(\camp),N} &:= "\1_{\hmactive_t \neq 0} \frac{1}{\hmactive_t}\sum_{m=1}^{\hmactive_t} \delta_{\camp_t^m} "
	\end{aligned}
\end{equation*}

A macroscopic description of this model for the amoebas' part is a density $\adens(t,\cdot)$ independent form $N$ such that, in some sense

\begin{equation*}
	\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N} \xrightarrow{N\longrightarrow +\infty} \adens(t,\cdot)
\end{equation*}

This description is macroscopic, because we loose the information about each individual, but we still describe the movement of the individuals as a whole.
In this chapter we first craft a candidate for such a density and an associated system of SDEs, and then we explain exactly in which sense do we require convergence of the measures above, and how to prove it.

\section{Convergence of empirical measures}

We follow \cite[Section 11.4]{dudley} and \cite[Part 1, Ch. 6]{cedric-villani-opt-trans}.
In general, having a sequence of probability measures $(\mu_N)_N$ and a probability measure $\mu$ on $\R^d$, the sequence is converging (weakly) to $\mu$ iff

\begin{equation*}
	\forall f \in \Cspace_b(\R^d). \qquad \mu_N(f) := \int f d\mu_N \xrightarrow{N} \mu(f) = \int f d\mu
\end{equation*}

\ie we see the space of probability measures $\PrbMeas(\R^d)$ as the dual space of $\Cspace_b(\R^d)$ endowed with the weak-* topology, where $\Cspace_b(\R^d)$ is the space of bounded continuous functions $f:E \longrightarrow \R$ endowed with the norm $\Norm{\cdot}_\infty$.
This definition cannot be directly applied to our case, because the empirical measures are \emph{random}, \ie are \rv mapping into the space of probability measures.
For this to be a valid definition we need to have a $\sigma$-algebra on $\PrbMeas(\R^d)$:

\begin{definition}
	The \emph{weak Borel $\sigma$-algebra on $\PrbMeas(\R^d)$} is the $\sigma$ algebra generated by the weak-* topology on $\PrbMeas(\R^d) = (\Cspace_b(\R^d))^\star$, \ie the coearset $\sigma$-algebra such that
		\begin{equation*}
			\forall f \in \BL(\R^d).\qquad
			\phi_f: \PrbMeas(\R^d) \longrightarrow \R_+, \qquad
			\phi_f(\mu) := \int f d\mu\quad
			\text{ is measurable}
		\end{equation*}
\end{definition}

Then the condition that we actually need to show is the convergence in law of the \rv $\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N}$ to the (constant) \rv $\adens_t$:

\begin{align*}
	\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N} \xrightarrow{\text{in law in $\PrbMeas(\R^d)$}} \adens_t
	\qquad \Longleftrightarrow
	\quad
	&\forall F\in \Cspace_b(\PrbMeas(\R^d)).\quad
	\E{F(\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N})}
	\xrightarrow{N \rightarrow \infty} 
	\E{F(\adens_t)}
\end{align*}

This condition is cumbersome, and we can do better than it.
Thanks to the theorem \cite[Proposition 2.2]{sznitman} we can find a less scary condition:

\begin{proposition}\label{ch3:proposition:sznitman}
	Let $\amba^{(N)} := (\amba^1, \ldots, \amba^N)$ be exchangeable\footnotemark[1] \rv in $\R^{dN}$, let $\Psi^N\in \PrbMeas(\R^{dN})$ be its law and $\Psi^{N, (k)}$ the $k$-th marginal, \ie
	\begin{equation*}
		\Psi^{N, (k)} (x) := \int_{\R^{d(N-k)}} \Psi^N dx_{k+1} \ldots dx_N
	\end{equation*}
	Let $\empmeas^{(\amba),N}$ its empirical measure.
	Then for a measure $\adens \in \PrbMeas(\R^d)$ are equivalent the following statements:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item The \rv $\empmeas^{(\amba),N}$ with values in $\PrbMeas(\R^d)$ converges in law to the constant \rv $\adens \in \PrbMeas(\R^d)$.
		\item For all $k \geq 1$ the marginal $\Psi^{N, (k)}$ converges weakly to $ \otimes_{p=1}^N \adens$ in $N$.
		\item The $2$-marginal $\Psi^{N, (2)}$ converges weakly to $ \adens\otimes\adens$ in $N$.
	\end{itemize}
\end{proposition}
\footnotetext[1]{Meaning that under each permutation $\pi$ the $(\amba^{\pi(1)}, \ldots, \amba^{\pi(N)})$ has the same law as $\amba^(N) := (\amba^1, \ldots, \amba^N)$, a condition fullfilled by the system \ref{ch2:model} for every $t$.}

In this way we must "only" show the weak convergence of the $k$-marginals $\Psi^{N, (k)}$ to $ \otimes_{p=1}^N \adens$.
In order to show that we need another instrument: a suitable metric for the topology of the weak convergence of measures (that, as we have seen, is the weak-* topology on $\Cspace_b(\R^d)$).


\subsection{A metric for the weak-convergence of $1$-Borel probability measures}

Now we need a concept of \emph{distance} between measures: the \emph{$p$-Wasserstein distance}, also known as \emph{$p$-Kantorovich–Rubinstein} or \emph{Earth-mover distance} (the latter one can be generalized to finite measures) because of the heuristic that we will see in \ref{ch3:fig:example-coupling}.
First we need the concept of \emph{coupling} of measures.

\begin{definition}
	Consider the space of Borel probability measures on $\R^u$
	\begin{equation}
		\BorelPrbMeas := \BorelPrbMeas (\R^u) := 
		\{\mu :
		\mu \text{ is a \emph{Borel} prob. meas. on } \R^u
		\}
	\end{equation}
	and let $\mu, \nu \in \PrbMeas$. A \emph{coupling of $\mu$ and $\nu$} is a measure $\gamma \in \PrbMeas (\R^u \times \R^u)$ such that for all Borel-measurable and bounded functions $\phi$ we have
	\begin{equation}
		\int_{\R^u \times \R^u} \phi(x) \,d\gamma(x,y) = \int_{\R^u} \phi(x) \,d\mu(x) 
		\quad \text{ and } \quad
		\int_{\R^u \times \R^u} \phi(y) \,d\gamma(x,y) = \int_{\R^u} \phi(y) \,d\nu(x) 
	\end{equation}\label{ch3:def-coupling}
	We sign as $\Gamma(\mu,\nu)$ the set of couplings of $\mu$ and $\nu$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
	The set $\Gamma(\mu,\nu)$ is never empty because the product measure $\mu \otimes \nu$ is always in it.
\end{remark}

A way to visualise a coupling is as a transformation of $\mu$ into $\nu$.
Assume, for the sake of simplicity, that $\mu,\nu$ are measures on $\R$ and are absolute continuous with respect to the Lebesgue measure $\lebsge$.
Consider the densities $\frac{d\mu}{d\lebsge}(x), \frac{d\nu}{d\lebsge}(x)$ and think of their graphs as piles of earth (see \ref{ch3:fig:example-coupling}).

\begin{figure}[h]
	\begin{tikzpicture}
	\end{tikzpicture}
	\caption{[[HERE DRAW A TIKZ THE EARTH MOVING STUFF INTO 2 DIMENSION]]}
\end{figure}\label{ch3:fig:example-coupling}

A plan to move earth in $\R$ can be thought as a function $\overline{\gamma}: \R \times \R \longrightarrow \R $ which with the value $\overline{\gamma}(x,y)=: \overline{\gamma}(x\rightarrow y)$ tells how much earth we should move from the point $x$ to the point $y$.
In order for this to be a plan which precisely transforms $\frac{d\mu}{d\lebsge}$ into $\frac{d\nu}{d\lebsge}$, it must respect two symmetrical conditions:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item For each $x\in\R$ the total earth (mass of) moved from it by the plan must be exactly equal to the earth present in $x$ at the beginning, namely:
	\begin{equation}
		\int \overline{\gamma}(x,y) \,dy = \frac{d\mu}{d\lebsge}(x)
	\end{equation}
	\item For each $y\in\R$ the earth (the mass) moved to it must be exactly equal to the earth present in $y$ at the end, namely:
	\begin{equation}
		\int \overline{\gamma}(x,y) \,dx = \frac{d\nu}{d\lebsge}(y)
	\end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

This is equivalent to \ref{ch3:def-coupling}, with $\overline{\gamma}$ playing the role of the density of the coupling $\gamma$.
With this example in mind we could ask ourselves which is the best among such transformations of $\mu$ into $\nu$.
To do that we need to associate to each coupling $\gamma \in \Gamma(\mu,nu)$ a value, which indicates how "good" a plan $\gamma$ might be.
If we have a notion of distance $dist$ on our space $\R^u$, then a plan can be evaluated with how much net distance we cover to transport all the earth around, \ie with the expression

\begin{equation}
	\int dist(x,y) \,d\gamma(x,y)
\end{equation}

In this way we can say that two measures $\mu,\nu$ are near to each others if we can find a coupling $\gamma \in \Gamma(\mu,\nu)$ such that its evaluation is very low.
With this idea in mind, choosing the distance induced by the normalised $1$-norm on $\R^u$ (meaning $\frac{|\cdot|_1}{u}$), one reaches the definition of the $1$-Wassertein distance (with respect to the normalised $1$-norm).

\begin{definition}
	Consider the space of Borel probability measures with finite $1$-moments on $\R^u$
	\begin{equation}
		\BorelPrbMeas_1 := \BorelPrbMeas_1(\R^u) := 
		\{\mu \in \BorelPrbMeas :
		\int |x| d \mu (x) < \infty
		\} \subseteq \BorelPrbMeas
	\end{equation}
	Then the $1$-Wasserstein distance between two measures $\mu,\nu \in \BorelPrbMeas_1$ is defined as
	\begin{equation}
		W_1(\mu,\nu):= \inf_{\gamma \in \Gamma(\mu,\nu)}
		\int_{\R^u \times \R^u} \frac{1}{u}\sum_{i=1}^{u} |x_i-y_i| d\gamma(x,y)
	\end{equation}
\end{definition}

To see the proof that $W_1$ is a distance take a look at the proof after \autocite[Example 6.3]{cedric-villani-opt-trans}.
The reason why we cover this concept is this result (from \autocite[Corollary 6.13]{cedric-villani-opt-trans}):

\begin{theorem}
	If $(X,d)$ is a Polish space, and $\overline{d}$ is a bounded distance inducing the same topology on $X$ as $d$, then the convergence of measures in the $1$-Wasserstein sense for $\overline{d}$ is equivalent to the usual weak convergence of probability measures in $\BorelPrbMeas_1(\R^u)$.
\end{theorem}

Then we have the subsequent statement:

\begin{lemma}\label{ch3:lemma-sznitman-wasserstein}
	Let $\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N},\adens_t, \Psi_t^{N, (k)},\Psi_t^{N}$ be as in \ref{ch3:proposition:sznitman} and let $\forall k\geq 1. \Psi_t^{N, (k)},\Psi_t^{N} \in \BorelPrbMeas_1$.
	Then the condition
	\begin{equation}\label{ch3:w-1-convergence-condition}
		\sup_{0\leq t\leq T}
		W_1 \big(
		\Psi_t^{N, (k)}, \bigotimes_{p=1}^{k}\adens_t
		\big),
		\quad
		\sup_{0\leq t\leq T}
		W_1 \big(
		\Psi_t^{N}, \bigotimes_{p=1}^{N}\adens_t
		\big),
		\xrightarrow {N \rightarrow \infty} 0
	\end{equation}
	implies that the \rv $\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N}$ with values in $\PrbMeas(\R^d)$ converges in law to the constant \rv $\adens_t \in \PrbMeas(\R^d)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{remark}
	Notice that the condition that $\forall k\geq 1. \Psi_t^{N, (k)},\Psi_t^{N} \in \BorelPrbMeas_1$ is not particularly strict, it translates to requiring that the amoebas do not have the possibility to have an infinite velocity.
\end{remark}

\begin{proof}
	By \ref{ch3:proposition:sznitman} we have that the condition
	\begin{equation*}
		\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N} \xrightarrow{\text{in law in $\PrbMeas(\R^d)$}} \adens_t
	\end{equation*}
	is equivalent to ask that for all $k \geq 1$, the marginals $\Psi^{N, (k)}$ converge weakly to $ \otimes_{p=1}^N \adens$ in $N$.
	Since for every $k\geq 1$ we have that $\Psi_t^{N, (k)} \in \BorelPrbMeas_1$, and since $\BorelPrbMeas_1$ is a Polish space, then also $\lim_N \Psi_t^{N, (k)} = \bigotimes_{p=1}^{k} \adens_t$  is an element of $\BorelPrbMeas_1$.
	Since the $1$-Wasserstein distance $W_1$ is a metric for the weak-convergence of measures, this implies that for every $k\geq 1$ we have that $\Psi_t^{N, (k)}$ converges weakly to $\bigotimes_{p=1}^{k} \adens_t$.
	By the proposition \ref{ch3:proposition:sznitman}, this implies that the \rv $\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N}$ converges in law to $\adens_t$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Effective equations and mean-field particles}

We want to find a good candidate $\adens_t := a(t, \cdot)$, but we have no analytical description of the solution of \ref{ch2:model}, we have only its evolution in time.
Effective equations are description of the evolution of the macroscopic description, and this is what realistically we are searching for, \ie a partial differential equation

\begin{equation*}
	\partial_t \adens_t = (\text{some function}) (t, \adens_t)
\end{equation*}

satisfied by $\adens_t$, the macroscopic description of our system.
More precisely, we expect this to happen up to the imposition of the same initial conditions, namely when we also impose that $\adens(0, \cdot) = \adens_0$, where $\adens_0$ is the density of $\amba_0^p$ imposed in the model \ref{ch2:model}.
This might work, but we do not know yet how to find a candidate, and even if we had one, it would still be cumbersome to work with it:
since we have no analytical solution for the model \ref{ch2:model}, we cannot work directly with the marginals $\Psi_t^N$, and we are thus stuck with the empirical measure

\begin{equation*}
\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N} := \frac{1}{N}\sum_{p=1}^N \delta_{\amba_t^p}
\end{equation*}

which is a discrete object.
On the other side we have $\adens_t$, which is a continuous guy.
Then we need a bridge from the PDE-world to the SDE-world: the Fokker-Planck equation (refer for example to \autocite[Theorem 8.1.1]{oksendal}).
Given an SDE

\begin{equation}\label{ch3:SDE-for-Fokker-Planck}
	\mfamba_t = \adens_0 + \int_0^t \drift(t, \mfamba_t) \,dt + \int_0^t \diffusion(t, X_t) \,d B_t
\end{equation}

if this has a solution $X_t$, then its density $\adens(t, \cdot)$ satifies the PDE

\begin{equation*}\label{ch3:fokker-planck-PDE}
	\left\{
		\begin{aligned}
			\partial_t \adens(t,x) 
			&=  - \sum_{i=1}^{d} \partial_{x_i}[ \drift^i(t,x) \adens(t,x)] 
			+ \sum_{i=1}^{d} \sum_{j=1}^{d} \frac{\partial^2}{\partial_{x_i}\partial_{x_j}} [D_{ij}(t,x) \adens(t,x)]
			\\
			\adens(0,x) &= \adens_0
			\\
			D_{ij}
			&:= \frac{1}{2} \sum_{k} \sigma^{ik}\sigma^{jk} = \frac{1}{2} (\sigma \sigma^T)_{ij}
		\end{aligned}
	\right.
\end{equation*}

The viceversa is also true: if we encounter a PDE of the form \ref{ch3:fokker-planck-PDE} with solution $\adens_t$, then we can observe the system \ref{ch3:SDE-for-Fokker-Planck} knowing that its solution has $\adens_t$ as density. 

The idea is to exploit this to build somehow a system of $N$ \iid particles 

\begin{equation}
	\mfamba_t^{(N)}=(\mfamba_t^p)_{p=1}^N
\end{equation}

which for every $t$ are always particularly close (in space) to the solutions $\amba_t^{(N)}=(\amba_t^p)_{p=1}^N$ of the system \ref{ch2:model} (the exact condition is exposed in the next statement).
In this way, if $\mfamba_t^{(N)}=(\mfamba_t^p)_{p=1}^N$ are \iid, the marginals of $\mfamba_t^{(N)}$ are exactly 
$
	\bigotimes_{p=1}^{N}\adens_t
$
and we then just need to show that this "closeness" in space for every time $t$ implies a closeness of the marginals of the two groups of particles, fullfilling \ref{ch3:lemma-sznitman-wasserstein}.
Formally we have this statement

\begin{lemma}
	Let  $\mfamba_t^{(N)}=(\mfamba_t^p)_{p=1}^N$ be a system of \iid particles such that the density of each $\mfamba_t^p$ is $\adens_t$.
	Suppose that the marginals $\Psi^{N, (k)}$ of $\Psi^{N}= \law(\amba_t^{(N)})$ are elements of $\BorelPrbMeas_1$.
	Suppose that there exists parameters $0 < \cutparameter < \xi < \frac{1}{2}$ and a constant $K > 0$ independent form $N$ such that
	\begin{equation}\label{ch3:condition-closeness}
		\PrbOf{\max_{1\leq p \leq N} \norm{\amba_t^p - \mfamba_t^p}_\infty > N^{-\cutparameter}
		}
		\leq\
		K\
		N^{-(\xi-\cutparameter)}
	\end{equation}
	Then we have that
	\begin{equation*}
		\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N} \xrightarrow{\text{in law in $\PrbMeas(\R^d)$}} \adens_t
	\end{equation*}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
	By the lemma \ref{ch3:lemma-sznitman-wasserstein}  it is sufficient to show that the condition \ref{ch3:condition-closeness} implies 
	\begin{equation*}
		\sup_{0\leq t\leq T}
		W_1 \big(
		\Psi_t^{N, (k)}, \bigotimes_{p=1}^{k}\adens_t
		\big),
		\quad
		\sup_{0\leq t\leq T}
		W_1 \big(
		\Psi_t^{N}, \bigotimes_{p=1}^{N}\adens_t
		\big),
		\xrightarrow {N \rightarrow \infty} 0
	\end{equation*}
	
	[[continue from here on, the proof is quite straightforward]]
\end{proof}

With this in mind we can imagine to search for a system of $N$ particles $\mfsol_t^N = (\mfamba_t^{(N)}, \mfcamp_t^{(N)})$ of which we easily know how to describe the law of by Fokker-Planck, but that remain close enough to the original system \ref{ch2:model}, up to imposing the same initial condition $\adens_0$.
Then we can set our candidate for the role of effective equation as the solution $\adens_t$ to its Fokker-Planck equation.
In fact intuitively, if we have such particles $\mfsol_t^N = (\mfamba_t^{(N)}, \mfcamp_t^{(N)})$ , since they remain always close enough to the original ones $\sol_t^N = (\amba_t^{(N)}, \camp_t^{(N)})$, we should expect their respective empirical densities 

\begin{equation*}
	\empmeas_t^{(\mfamba),N} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{p=1}^N \delta_{\mfamba_t^p},\qquad 
	\empmeas_t^{(\amba),N} := \frac{1}{N}\sum_{p=1}^N \delta_{\amba_t^p}
\end{equation*}

to be close, and thus tend to the same limit in $N$, which we would expect to be the same one given by the Fokker Planck equation.
More practically we want that;
\begin{enumerate}
	\item The particles $\mfsol_t^N = (\mfamba_t^{(N)}, \mfcamp_t^{(N)})$ should be coupled with their counterparts $\sol_t^N = (\amba_t^{(N)}, \camp_t^{(N)})$ (\ie they should have the same \Bm in their stochastic evolution), in order to control more easily the distance between the two.
	Indeed in such case the distance process $\sol_t^N -\mfsol_t^N$ is a finite variation process and can be thus more easily treated, depending only on the two drifts, and not on the diffusions.
	\item The single particles $\mfamba_t^1, \ldots, \mfamba_t^N$ must be \iid so that on each one of them can be described by the same SDE and thus we can use Fokker-Planck separately to have $N$ identical densities $\adens_t^p = \adens_t$.
	This is necessary to be sure that the empirical measure $\empmeas_t^{(\mfamba),N} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{p=1}^N \delta_{\mfamba_t^p}$ tends to the $\adens_t$ in the sense that will be described in the next section.
	\item Under the conditional probability $\PrbCondTimes{\cdot}{}$ the particles $\mfcamp_t^1, \ldots, \mfcamp_t^{\hmactive_t}$ should be at least indipendent.
	It would be desirable to have them \iid to easily apply Fokker-Planck also in this case, but this is not possible, because we need the distance process $\sol_t^N - \mfsol_t^N$ to be well defined and this can happen only if the two process $\mfsol_t^N$ and $\sol_t^N$ have the same dimension at each time $t$.
	Besides, they must also be coupled with their respective counterparts $(\camp_t^m)_m$, which depends on quasi-\Bms starting at random times.
	For this reason $\mfcamp_t^1, \ldots, \mfcamp_t^{\hmactive_t}$ must be created at same random times as their counterparts, but must be at least independent.
\end{enumerate}

For now these were parameters on the movement of such particles, but we still have not pointed out at the main ingredient of our recipe: the idea is that the macroscopic behaviour is dictated by the \emph{average} dynamic of the system, more precisely, by the \emph{mean} field felt by each amoeba $\amba_t^p$ (and analogously for the cAMP $\camp_t^m$):

\begin{equation*}
	\mfdrift_p^N(t, x) := \ECondTimes{\drift_p^N(t, \mfsol_t) }{\mfsol_t^p = x}
\end{equation*}

Then the candidate for our system would be something of the form

\begin{equation}\label{eq:approximate-mean-field-equations}
	\left\{
	\begin{aligned}
		\mfamba^p_t &= \int_0^t \mfdrift^N_p(s,\mfamba_s^k) d s + \sigma_\amba B_t^{\mfamba,k} &
		\\
		\mfcamp^m_t &= \text{ (some spawning position } P_{\btime_m}^m)) + \sigma_\camp Q_t^m\qquad &\text{from time }\btime_m
	\end{aligned}
	\right.
\end{equation}

where, to enforce the independence of the different particles, we ask that the spawning positions $P_{\btime_m}^m$ are independent from each others and from the $\mfamba^p$.
We call such particles the \emph{mean-field particles}, for obvious reasons.





























































Effective equations are the macroscopic description of the system.
The idea is that part of the very complex behaviour of many bodies interacting with each others can be averaged out on a bigger scale, leaving thus only the essential part of the interactions.
We start from a cinematic $\sol_t = (\amba_t, \camp_t) \in (\R^d)^{\NumParticles_t}$, the solution to one of the models, with initial condition

\begin{equation}
\amba_0 \sim \adens_0 \otimes \ldots \otimes \adens_0
\end{equation}

for some distribution $\adens_0 \in \Lpspace^1 \cap \Lpspace^\infty$.
We search for a system of PDEs

\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
\partial_t \adens(t, x) &= \ldots
\\
\partial_t \cdens(t, x) &= \ldots
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}

such that the its solutions $\adens, \cdens: \text{time} \times \text{space} \longrightarrow \R_+ $ (under the same initial condition,\ie $\adens(0,\cdot)=\adens_0$) represent faithfully (in some sense yet to be explained) respectively the density of the amoebas and of the cAMP molecules when the number of amoebas $N$ tends to infinity.

In order to formalise the definition, we must understand that the number of initial amoebas $N$ is here relevant, therefore we sign

\begin{equation}
\sol_t = \sol_t^N = ( \sol_t^{N,k})_k \in (\R^d )^{\NumParticles_t} = (\R^d)^{\NumParticles_{N,t}}
\end{equation}

as the solution to the model with $N$ amoebas.
Consider now $\Psi_t^{\amba,N}:=\law(\amba_t), \Psi_t^{\camp,N}:=\law(\camp_t)$, \ie the law of the amoeba's and the cAMP's parts of the solution $\sol_t^N$.
Consider its $(k)$-th marginals

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
{}^{(k)}\Psi_t^{\amba,N}
&:= \int_{\R^{d(N-k)}} \Psi_t^{\amba,N} \, dx_{k+1} \ldots dx_N
\\
{}^{(k)}\Psi_t^{\camp,N}
&:= \int_{\R^{d(\NumParticles-k)}} \Psi_t^{\camp,N} \, dx_{k+1} \ldots dx_{\NumParticles}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Then we can formalise the notion of faithful representation, at least for the amoebas' part, as the condition that for each $t\geq 0, k \geq 1$ we have that ${}^{(k)}\Psi_t^{\amba,N}$ converge weakly to $\otimes_{p=1}^{N}\adens_t$ (for $N$ going to $\infty$), \ie in symbols

\begin{equation}
\forall t\geq 0, k \geq 1 \quad
{}^{(k)}\Psi_t^{\amba,N} \rightharpoonup \bigotimes_{p=1}^{k}\adens_t
\end{equation}

It will be more comfortable to operate in a bounded time-interval $[0, T]$ for an arbitrary $T > 0$, and thus the equivalent condition will become

\begin{equation}
\forall 0\leq t\leq T, k \geq 1 \quad
{}^{(k)}\Psi_t^{\amba,N} \rightharpoonup \bigotimes_{p=1}^{k}\adens_t
\end{equation}

\section{How make a smart guess for the effective equations and prove that it is the correct choice}

\begin{remark}
	Here the number of amoebas involved in the model starts to be relevant, thus both for stochastic processes $\sol_t$ and functions $\drift$ dependent on it, such number will be signed as a superscript: $\sol_t^N, \:\drift^N$.
	This shall not create ambiguity, in the case in which we want to sign the components of a multivariate stochastic process $\sol_t^N$: they shall be $\sol_t^{N,p,g}$.
	A further element to avoid confusion is that the number of amoebas will always use the variable name $N$.
\end{remark}

Instead of guessing the PDE, the idea is to guess the equivalent-mean field particles $\mfsol = (\mfamba^1, \ldots, \mfamba^N, \mfcamp^1, \ldots, \mfcamp^{\hmactive_t})$ solutions to a system of equations 

\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
\mfamba^p_t &= \int_0^t \mfdrift^{N}_p(s,\mfamba_s^p) d s + \sigma_A B_t^p &
\\
\mfcamp^{p,g}_t &= \text{ (some spawning position } \mfspawnpos_{\btime^{p,g}}^{p,g})) + \int_0^t \mfdrift_p^N(s,\mfcamp_s^p) + \sigma_C \quasibm_t^{p,g} &\text{from time }\btime^{p,g}
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}\label{ch3:eq:approximate-mean-field-equations}

where the drift terms $\mfdrift^{N,k}$ are calculated by taking the average of the original $\drift^p$s, \ie

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mfdrift_p^N(t, x) &:= \ECond{\drift^N_p(t, \mfsol_t)}{ \mfsol_t^p} (x)
\\
\mfdrift_{p,g}^{N}(t, x) &:= \ECond{\drift_{p,g}^N(t, \mfsol_t)}{ \mfsol_t^{p,g} } (x)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

and all the spawning positions $\mfspawnpos_{\btime^{p,g}}^{p,g}$ are independent from each others and from the $A^p$s.
Then one considers the correspondent Kolmogorov forward equation, and that is the effective equation searched for.
The idea behind this guess is that with a big number of amoebas and of cAMP molecules, their \emph{drift field} should resemble more and more the \emph{average drift field}, due to the fact that there are many long range interactions.
For this reason the choice of the name \emph{mean-field particles}.

\begin{remark}
\begin{enumerate}
\item We will also couple this system with the solution of \ref{ch2:model}, meaning that we will take the same Poisson processes $(\hmactive^p)_p$ (thus birth times) and the same \Bm of such solution.
This is made in order to express the distance between the mean-field particles and the original ones in an easier way, namely for each coordinate

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
| \sol_t^k - \mfsol_t^k | 
&= \left| 
		\int_0^t \drift_{k}^{N}(s,\sol_s) d s + \sigma B_t^k - \int_0^t \mfdrift_k^{N}(t,\mfsol_s^k) d s + \sigma B_t^k 
	\right|
\\
&= 
	\left| \int_0^t \drift_k^{N}(s,\sol_s)  - \mfdrift_k^{N}(t,\mfsol_s^k) d s
	\right|
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\item Notice that, while all the $\mfamba_t^p$ are \emph{iid}, the cAMP mean-fiel particles $\mfcamp_t^p$ are only \emph{independent} (\emph{not identically distributed}) and only \emph{under the conditioned probability} $ \PrbCondTimes{\cdot}{} $.
And it cannot be otherwise because of the coupling: indeed for each $\camp^{p,g}$ such quasi-\Bm $\quasibm^{p,g}_t$ starts to differ from $0$ only at $\btime^{p,g}$, and the molecule must necessary spawn at such time.
The disadvantage of not having identical distributions, is that crafting a law of large numbers becomes more difficult.
\end{enumerate}
\end{remark}

\subsection{Calculations}

First we notice that since the drift of the cAMP molecules is $\equiv 0$, their average drift field is also 0, \ie

\begin{align*}
\forall p,g. \quad \mfdrift_{p,g}^{N}(t, \mfsol_t^{p,g}) 
:&= \ECond {\drift_{p,g}^{N}(t, \mfsol_t) }{\mfsol_t^{p,g}}

&=\ECond {\ \ECondTimes{ \drift_{p,g}^{N}(t, \mfsol_t) }{\mfsol_t^{p,g}, }\ }{\mfsol_t^{p,g}}

\end{align*}

\subsection{Does the system have a solution? With which properties?}

The system \ref{ch3:integro-differential-system} is a non-linear integro-differential equation.
The best instrument to prove that it does possess a solution is the Schauder fixed point theorem (see \autocite[Exercise 6.26]{brezis}, which does not guarantee unicity:

\begin{theorem}
Let $E$ be a Banach space and let $C \subseteq E$ be a non-empty closed convex subset of $E$.
Let $F: C \longrightarrow C$ be a $\Cspace^0$ map such that $F(C) \subseteq K$, where $K\subseteq C$ is a compact subset of $C$.
Then $F$ has a fixed point in $K$.
\end{theorem}

The usual procedure to apply the theorem is to divide the linear part from the non-linear one, to invert the linear operator with the green function of the linear operator, and then to verify that, with a domain in the space of functions with the characteristics that we do desire, the operator respects the conditions of the fixed point schauder theorem.
